Market size (FLAG: vendor/market-research figures, big variance — treat as directional, not gospel):

What buyers ACTUALLY want (the on-brand signal — anti-hype aligns with market):
- ROI-focused: "53% of investors expect positive ROI in six months or less." Buyers spend where pain is obvious and return is measurable: customer support, sales ops, finance workflows, reporting, document processing, service delivery.
- Measurable results over vague promises: "Companies are no longer impressed by vague promises or disconnected pilots. They want partners who can improve a real workflow, integrate with existing systems, reduce risk, and show measurable ROI."
- Outcome-based pricing demand: pay for a result (resolved support request, completed workflow, qualified lead, processed document), not just tool access.
- HYBRID consultant-developer model prized: "Someone who understands the business, talks to the team and builds solutions live, adjusting in real time... half consultant, half developer." (Directly favors Nick = software engineer.)

Credible vs HYPE (this IS the brand thesis, sourced):
- Hype problem: many orgs stuck in pilot mode. "Great demos, impressive proofs of concept, zero enterprise-wide impact."
- Overpromise on autonomy: "58% of deployed agents require manual override 20-40% of the time in production, despite vendor claims of 'lights-out automation'. Most teams see 20-35% time reduction in year one, not 80%." (FLAG: stat from aifire/automaton blog, not peer-reviewed — credible-sounding but verify.)
- Winners (VERBATIM): "The winners won't be the vendors with the boldest autonomy claims. They'll be the ones who delivered reliable agents on realistic scope, with transparent error metrics, and honest communication about what they can't yet do."

Realistic ROI (FLAG: blog-sourced ranges, treat as illustrative):
- ~280-520% first-year ROI for focused small-business deployments; ~330% over 3 yrs for enterprise intelligent automation.
- Bimodal distribution: "20% of adopters capture 75% of the gains" — do it well and win, or half-heartedly and end up worse.

Market segmentation opportunity for a solo/small shop:
- Mid-market ($1-50M revenue) can't afford $50k-$200k engagements but need sophisticated automation. They want senior expertise + fast execution, not junior consultants learning on their dime.

## SOURCE: Search synthesis — specialist vs generalist consultant positioning
Sources: Consulting Success "Why Specialization is Essential"; Gatilab niche selection; Sarah Moon "Consulting Niches 2026"; Melisa Liberman; Casebasix generalist vs specialist.
Accessed: 2026-06-19

Why specialist beats generalist:
- Pricing power: niche expert charges premium; when you're the obvious expert, price sensitivity drops.
- Competitive advantage: compete only within your niche (smaller field), not all consultants.
- Faster recognition: prospects recognize you as the expert faster, less convincing needed.
- Marketing efficiency: speak to specific problems in the niche's exact terminology vs generic messaging that resonates with no one.

"Expert in X for Y" / niche formula (VERBATIM): "Right niche + Signature Style + Strong Process + Premium Proof = High-ticket clients who are READY to buy."

Generalist trap: clients increasingly seek specialized expertise + a trustworthy personal brand; generalists get overlooked for those with specific industry/functional knowledge.
