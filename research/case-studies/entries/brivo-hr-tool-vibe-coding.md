## What they did
Hans Kahler, COO of Brivo (an access-control/security company), says his HR leader asked
for "a better system for managing quarterly objectives and accomplishments": a place for
employees to record goals, managers to get visibility, and HR to report across
departments. After pricing enterprise HR software, Kahler says he built the tool himself
"in a few days" instead of buying. The account is a first-person LinkedIn post.

## The numbers
- Avoided "close to $100K" in HR software spend, once licensing, onboarding, and scaling
  across the company were factored in. (Self-reported by the COO.)
- Build time: "a few days." (Self-reported.)
- No headcount, stack details, or maintenance cost given in the captured excerpt; the post
  tail is behind LinkedIn's login wall.

## What broke / the caveats
This is a self-reported social-media post with no invoice, demo, or third-party
confirmation. The "$100K avoided" is a projected/quoted price, not a paid-then-cancelled
bill. No detail on what the tool was built with, whether it touches sensitive HR data
safely, or who maintains it. Treat the number as directional at best.

## Why it matters for our beat
A named COO at a named (security) company building an internal HR tool himself is a clean
example of the pattern moving up the org chart: the builder here is an executive, not an
intern, which matches the Retool finding that most shadow builders are senior. The irony
that a security company's COO ships an internal tool without (publicly) noting the security
review is itself useful texture for the "second bill" angle.

## Post angles
- A one-line example in the "non-engineers building tools" piece: the builder is the COO,
  not the intern. Use with the saving labeled clearly as self-reported.
- Feeds a synthesis on who actually builds (senior managers, per Retool) and what that
  means for governance.
