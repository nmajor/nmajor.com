# Why a Right to Explanation Does Not Exist in the GDPR — Wachter, Mittelstadt & Floridi (2017)

**Citation:** Sandra Wachter, Brent Mittelstadt & Luciano Floridi, "Why a Right to
Explanation of Automated Decision-Making Does Not Exist in the General Data Protection
Regulation," *International Data Privacy Law*, Vol. 7, No. 2 (May 2017), pp. 76–99.

**Source:** https://academic.oup.com/idpl/article-abstract/7/2/76/3860948 ; SSRN
https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2903469

## Core argument

Widely claimed that GDPR mandates a "right to explanation" of automated decisions. The
authors argue this is doubtful: GDPR (Arts. 13–15) mandates only that data subjects receive
"meaningful, but properly limited, information ... about the logic involved" — a **"right to
be informed,"** not a genuine right to an explanation of a *specific* decision. They call
the Art. 22 "right not to be subject to automated decision-making" ambiguous and limited in
scope, and warn the GDPR "runs the risk of being toothless" on this point.

## Mapping to Nick's thesis (PARTIAL — mainly a caveat / honesty check)

Use this to *avoid overclaiming* about GDPR Art. 22. It is the counterweight that keeps the
Art. 22 citation honest: even a strong-sounding legal right can be hollow in practice, which
ironically reinforces Nick's deeper point — a paper transparency/appeal right is not the
same as *accountability*, i.e., a human who is answerable. It also motivates why the
answer may need to be *refusal* of certain automated decisions, not just a bolt-on
"right to explanation."
