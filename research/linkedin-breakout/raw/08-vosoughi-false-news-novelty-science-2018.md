# Vosoughi, Roy & Aral, "The spread of true and false news online" (Science, 2018) — the Novelty Hypothesis

Sources:
- Paper PDF (primary): https://politics.media.mit.edu/papers/Vosoughi_Science.pdf
- MIT News summary: https://news.mit.edu/2018/study-twitter-false-news-travels-faster-true-stories-0308
- Sinan Aral (author) essay on the novelty mechanism: https://sinanaral.medium.com/fake-news-about-our-fake-news-study-spread-faster-than-its-truth-just-as-we-predicted-77db6d9ca8c8

Citation: Vosoughi, S., Roy, D., & Aral, S. (2018). "The spread of true and false
news online." Science, 359(6380), 1146-1151. (Cover story; Altmetric's #2 most
influential scientific publication of 2018 across all disciplines.)

## Method + headline result
~126,000 verified true/false rumor cascades, tweeted ~4.5M times by ~3M people,
2006-2017, classified using six independent fact-checking orgs (95-98% agreement).
- **"Falsehood diffused significantly farther, faster, deeper, and more broadly than
  the truth in ALL categories of information."** False news was **~70% more likely to
  be retweeted** than the truth. The top 0.01% of false cascades diffused 8 hops
  deeper than the truth. (True news rarely reached >1,000 people; false news readily
  reached 1,000-100,000.)
- The effect was strongest for **political** news, then urban legends, business,
  terrorism, science, entertainment, natural disasters.

## The mechanism — the Novelty Hypothesis (this is the reusable, honest part)
The authors do NOT conclude "lies are good." They isolate WHY false news spread: it
was more NOVEL and evoked stronger emotion.
- **Novelty:** false rumors were significantly more novel than the true news users
  had been exposed to in the prior 60 days. "Human attention is drawn to novelty, we
  gain in status by spreading novel information because we are seen as being 'in the
  know' or having access to inside information that may surprise our friends... We
  are quick to react to information that is novel, surprising, shocking and anger
  inducing." (Aral) — note the tie-in to social currency (file 03) and arousal
  (file 01).
- **Emotion:** replies to false news showed more **surprise and disgust**; replies to
  true news showed more sadness, anticipation, and trust. (Surprise = high-arousal
  activation → sharing, consistent with Berger & Milkman.)
- Careful caveat from the authors (verbatim): they "cannot claim that novelty causes
  retweets" by itself — it is correlational — but the surprise profile fits the
  novelty account.

## The honest lesson for an anti-hype brand
The takeaway is NOT "be false." It is that **novelty and surprise are the diffusion
fuel, and truth's disadvantage is that it is usually LESS novel.** So the strategic
move for a truthful brand is to compete on the ONE axis where truth can still be
novel: **be genuinely FIRST, or genuinely surprising-but-true.** A verified,
first-of-its-kind real event (the first-ever SEC 8-K over shadow AI) is maximally
novel AND true — it neutralizes falsehood's usual novelty advantage. This is the
single most actionable reconciliation of "virality favors lies" with "we only tell
the truth": hunt for true novelty (first-of-kind, named, surprising-but-real
incidents), because that is where honest content can travel as far as bait.
