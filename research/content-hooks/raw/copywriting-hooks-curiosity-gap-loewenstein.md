ORIGINATOR: George Loewenstein, behavioral economist (Carnegie Mellon). Seminal 1994
Psychological Bulletin paper. This is the ACADEMIC basis behind the marketing term
"curiosity gap."

## THE CORE THEORY (verbatim / near-verbatim)

Loewenstein's definition (the most-cited line, paraphrased consistently across
sources): curiosity is
"a cognitively induced deprivation that arises from the perception of a gap in
knowledge and understanding."

Mechanism: Curiosity is a DRIVE STATE, analogous to hunger. A gap between what one
knows and what one wants to know creates a feeling of DEPRIVATION; that aversive
feeling MOTIVATES information-seeking to close the gap. Closing it is rewarding;
satiation occurs once enough information is consumed.

## THE CRITICAL INSIGHT FOR HOOKS (the "reference point" requirement)

Curiosity arises "when attention becomes focused on a gap in one's knowledge" — NOT
from general ignorance, but from awareness of a SPECIFIC, BOUNDED absence.

KEY MECHANISM (verbatim summary across sources):
"A small amount of information serves as a priming dose that greatly increases
curiosity."

And the crucial corollary:
"Complete ignorance does not produce curiosity; partial knowledge that reveals a
specific gap does. The more precisely the gap is identified, the more motivating it is."

PRACTICAL TRANSLATION FOR OPENINGS:
1. You must give the reader ENOUGH to make them aware of a specific thing they don't
   know. (You can't make someone curious about something they can't even conceive of.)
2. The gap must feel BOUNDED and CLOSEABLE — "I'm 90% there, I just need the last
   piece" is the most motivating state. A vague "you won't BELIEVE what happened" is
   weaker than a precise, named gap.
3. The reader must already CARE about the domain (the gap must touch existing knowledge
   / a held question).

## RELATED RESEARCH
- Kang et al. (2009, "The Wick in the Candle of Learning," Psychological Science,
  Kang, Hsu, ... Loewenstein, Camerer): used fMRI on trivia questions. Found curiosity
  peaked at INTERMEDIATE confidence (when people felt close to knowing but didn't),
  curiosity activated reward circuitry (caudate), and high curiosity improved memory of
  the answer. Confirms the "almost-know" sweet spot.
- Golman & Loewenstein (2016): information gaps can both COMPOUND curiosity AND produce
  FRUSTRATION/aversion — i.e., an unresolved or manipulative gap can backfire and
  irritate. Relevant to why clickbait erodes trust.

## ANTI-HYPE ASSESSMENT — "the curiosity gap done honestly"
The information-gap theory itself is morally neutral; it's HOW you use it that
separates credible from clickbait:

CREDIBLE / HONEST CURIOSITY GAP (transfers well):
- Open a SPECIFIC, real, named question the reader genuinely shares ("Most teams
  measure model accuracy. Almost none can tell you what a 2-point accuracy gain is
  worth in dollars — here's how to.").
- The gap is REAL and the piece actually CLOSES it. Payoff matches the promise.
- Built on the reader's existing knowledge; respects their intelligence.

CLICKBAIT / TOO-SALESY (avoid):
- Withholding for its own sake; "you won't believe #7"; vague intrigue with no real
  payoff; manufactured mystery.
- These exploit the deprivation drive but DON'T close the gap honestly → per Golman &
  Loewenstein, this produces frustration and destroys trust with a skeptical audience.

RULE OF THUMB FOR THE BRAND: open a gap you will genuinely close, that a smart reader
actually has, stated specifically. That is the legitimate, durable version of the hook.
