Choice Home Warranty refused to pay the $3,000 its chatbot agreed to. The excuse: "we've had this AI up for about a week."

It's becoming a genre.

BMW Toronto, on a $27,162.79 offer nobody had authorised: "the AI bot ran with that, misunderstood the message." Cursor, after its support bot invented a subscription policy out of nothing: "an incorrect response from a front-line AI support bot." Air Canada told a tribunal it wasn't liable for what its own chatbot said, and declined to explain why.

Walmart has skipped the argument and written it into its terms. Pricing from its AI features "does not constitute an offer to sell the item at the stated price."

Now look one step up the chain, because the model providers are pointing too. Mistral's binding commitment to Italy's competition regulator was to put a line in the chat window: "Le Chat may make mistakes. Please check responses."

And the regulators are pointing straight back down. The UK's competition regulator says you're responsible for an AI agent the same way you're responsible for an employee, even if someone else built it. Utah's consumer protection director put it plainer: "'The chatbot did it' is not going to be an excuse."

So the company blames the bot. The bot's maker tells you to check its work. The regulator blames the company. And the customer is out $3,000.

Everyone in that chain has an answer except the person holding the bill.

This isn't only about bots quoting prices, either. If a model is triaging claims or scoring applications, nobody's negotiating a number, but a wrong output still lands on somebody.

So how do you get the benefit of these tools without taking on that kind of liability?
