BMW Toronto's chatbot quoted a customer $27,162.79. Nobody had authorised that number, and they only paid it after CBC News called.

Zack Giacomelli was selling his BMW back to the dealership he'd bought it from. A rep called Quinn texted him and offered $27,162.79, which was exactly what he still owed on the car. He'd walk away clear.

"I will come out at zero," he told CBC. "I'm like, this is happening."

He countered at $28,500. Quinn said that "sounds reasonable," then booked the meeting: "Let's lock in today at 3:30."

Before the meeting a human called. The offer wasn't valid. Quinn was an AI, and the price was a mistake. The real number was about $20,000.

Giacomelli took his texts to CBC. After CBC contacted the dealership, it reinstated the full amount.

The cause was mundane. An employee miscommunicated the payoff figure and, per the sales manager, "the AI bot ran with that."

No hallucination. Somebody got a number wrong the way people do all day, and the machine gave it authority. It named a price, agreed to a counter, and booked a signing.

Now count what could have caught it. Not the employee. Not a price check before the appointment. Not the rep who phoned, whose job that day was to take the offer back.

A reporter did.

If one of your systems committed you to something nobody authorised, how would you find out?
