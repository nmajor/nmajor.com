# LinkedIn Hook Mechanics — "See More" Cutoff & Character Limits

Sources:
- https://authoredup.com/blog/linkedin-character-limit (AuthoredUp, analysis of 372,126 posts Sep 2025–Feb 2026)
- Search aggregation across: connectsafely.ai, lettercounter.org, redactai.io, justpollen.com
Accessed: 2026-06-19

## The "See More" truncation (CORE MECHANIC)
- **Desktop:** roughly **210 characters** show before the "...see more" prompt (some sources say up to ~250).
- **Mobile:** roughly **140 characters** show before cutoff. (Some sources say mobile also ~210; there is variation by device/app version and formatting.)
- Hard post limit: **3,000 characters** (incl. spaces, line breaks, emojis).
- "Your first 210 characters decide whether anyone reads the rest."
- Practical takeaway: front-load the most compelling content BEFORE the cutoff. The hook = everything visible above the "see more" fold.

## Line breaks
- LinkedIn collapses the post after the first ~2-3 lines / ~210 chars (desktop). Using deliberate line breaks within those first lines lets you "use up" the visible space with a punchy one-liner + white space, pushing "see more" earlier and increasing curiosity (the post LOOKS like it continues).
- Jasmin Alić's read of LinkedIn's own UI: "Write the entire post in the first 3 lines" — because LinkedIn's preview shows ~3 lines incl. spaces. (See separate Jasmin file.)

## Optimal post length (AuthoredUp data, 372,126 posts)
- **Sweet spot: 1,301–2,500 characters** generates highest engagement.
- Engagement lift: "27% higher engagement than posts under 400 characters."
- Best-performing range: 2.61–2.67% median engagement rate in the 1,301–2,500 bracket.

## Format engagement benchmark (SocialInsider, cited)
- Carousels (documents): ~24.42% engagement rate.
- Text posts: ~6.67%.
- (Note: this is about FORMAT not hook wording, but relevant — carousels still need a cover hook.)

## CREDIBILITY NOTE
- The 210/140 character cutoff is a real platform mechanic, repeatedly confirmed — CREDIBLE and actionable.
- AuthoredUp length data is from a named tool with a stated sample size and window — relatively CREDIBLE (vendor data, but specific and large-N).
- The "340% more engagement from strong hooks" figure circulating on SEO blogs has NO traceable primary source — treat as folklore.
