# Seed — the AI disclosure gap (insurance shouts, banks stay silent)

## Thesis (one sentence)
The reason insurers talk about AI far more than banks isn't that banks use less of it —
it's that for a bank, admitting AI in writing is a liability, so the industry burning the
most compute is the one saying the least about it.

## Why a reader should care (one line)
If you run a regulated business, what you *say* about your AI and what you *run* are now two
different decisions with different risks — and getting that split wrong is its own exposure.

## Tactical take-home (woven, never labeled)
Before you write (or approve) a public word about your AI — a filing, a press release, a
careers page — ask what each claim commits you to if it's wrong. The bank move isn't
silence for its own sake; it's matching disclosure to liability. The reader should leave
auditing the gap between their own AI talk and their AI reality.

## Shape
News-anchored analysis (news hook → data → the "why" → the take). Nick's read is the point.

## Spine material (the concrete bits)
- Institute 10-K census (Nick's sister org): insurance 82.9% mention AI in 2025 10-Ks
  (above tech 77.4%); banks & lenders 22.8% — dead last of all traditional sectors.
  n=2,112. Its own caveat: measures *willingness to disclose*, not what runs.
- News hook: banks quietly racing to cut AI token bills — PNC building its own GPU compute;
  JPMorgan CDO: some employees "spend more on tokens than their salary"; RBC token use
  +500% YoY (American Banker, 2026-06-29).
- Banks are heavy real users: JPMorgan 450+/500+ production use cases, LLM Suite used by
  200k+ staff daily; AML false-positive reductions. (So low disclosure ≠ low use.)
- Why banks stay quiet: fair-lending liability (ECOA/FHA disparate impact), model-risk
  supervision (Fed SR 11-7), and AI-washing securities litigation punishing *over*-claiming.
- Why insurers talk: insurance IS prediction (underwriting/claims/fraud), AND state rules
  now *require* documenting AI — NAIC 2023 model bulletin, Colorado SB 205. Disclosure is
  partly forced, not just eager.
- Nick's frame: I build these systems for a living; the interesting line isn't "who uses
  AI" (everyone does now) but "who can afford to say so."

  adoption scale — the "banks use as much or more" leans on one firm (Evident). MUST frame
  22.8% as disclosure, or it reads as "banks barely use AI." Soften any hard adoption-delta claim.
- BCG "insurance leads AI adoption" page 403'd on fetch — corroborate before citing, or use
  Conning/NAIC instead.
