## AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
============================================================

ORIGINATOR: Elias St. Elmo Lewis, ~1898 (Philadelphia advertising course). Inducted
into the Advertising Hall of Fame. (Historiographic note: Carleton paper argues the
attribution to Lewis is partly overstated/weakly evidenced, but Lewis is the
conventional origin.) Lewis said he drew the idea from the psychology of William James.

LEWIS'S ORIGINAL FORMULATION (verbatim):
"The mission of an advertisement is to attract a reader, so that he will look at the
advertisement and start to read it; then to interest him, so that he will continue to
read it; then to convince him, so that when he has read it he will believe it."

Original slogan (1898): "Attract attention, maintain interest, create desire" — later
he added "get action."

THE HOOK ROLE: AIDA puts ATTENTION first and treats it as a FUNNEL — you cannot skip a
stage. The opening's only job is to win attention and earn the next sentence. Gary
Halbert taught AIDA explicitly: "First, get his attention; Second, get him interested;
Third, make him desire what you are selling; [Fourth] Compel him to take action."

## PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve (sometimes Problem-Agitate-Solution)
============================================================

POPULARIZED BY: Dan Kennedy ("father of modern direct-response copywriting"), notably
in The Ultimate Sales Letter. Dan Kennedy reportedly called PAS "the most reliable
copywriting formula for sales ever invented."

THE STRUCTURE:
1. PROBLEM — state the problem the reader feels.
2. AGITATE — poke/twist the problem until it's visceral; push it to its consequences.
3. SOLVE — present the solution (your product/idea) as the relief.

DAN KENNEDY EXAMPLE (verbatim, from a letter to husbands):
- Problem: "Women are different than we are. Your loving wife needs, wants, and
  deserves special attention maybe more often than you think to give it to her."
- Agitate: "You are busy. Preoccupied with work. Aggravated with that dumb dumb that
  you have to deal with every day at the office."
- (then) Solve.

WHY IT WORKS (the psychology): loss aversion. Per Kahneman & Tversky, people have
greater aversion to pain/loss than attraction to equivalent gain. PAS leads with the
PAIN the reader is already feeling — naming it precisely creates recognition ("they get
me") and the relief of the solution is felt more strongly than a pure-gain pitch.

## THE 4 U's — Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific
============================================================

ORIGINATOR: Michael Masterson (pen name of Mark Ford), American Writers & Artists Inc.
(AWAI). A checklist for headlines, subject lines, and openings. Rate each opening 1-4
on each U; revise the weak ones.

THE FOUR U's:
1. USEFUL — Does it promise a clear benefit / answer / solution to the reader? (Ranked
   most important: a headline must be useful for the reader to keep reading.)
2. URGENT — Does it give a reason to act/read NOW, not later?
3. UNIQUE — Is it said in a fresh way, or presenting a fresh idea? Does it stand out?
4. ULTRA-SPECIFIC — Is it concrete and precise (specific numbers, named details)
   rather than vague?

Masterson's priority order: USEFUL first, then URGENT, then UNIQUE, then ULTRA-SPECIFIC
(though specificity reinforces all the others).

## ANTI-HYPE ASSESSMENT
============================================================
- TRANSFERS WELL: PAS (when the problem is named honestly and not exaggerated — Crazy
  Egg's own framing is "How to use PAS without being cringy," i.e. agitate truthfully,
  don't fear-monger); USEFUL + UNIQUE + ULTRA-SPECIFIC from the 4 U's (these are pure
  substance/clarity levers); AIDA's discipline that the opening must earn attention
  honestly before anything else.
- HANDLE WITH CARE: URGENT (the weakest fit) — real deadlines/timeliness are fine, but
  MANUFACTURED/FAKE urgency ("only 3 spots left!", fake countdowns) is exactly the
  manipulative tactic a skeptical decision-maker audience punishes. Over-AGITATION in
  PAS can read as fear-mongering; keep it true and proportionate.
