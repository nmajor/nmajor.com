## What they did
The Warehouse Group, New Zealand's largest general-merchandise retailer, ran high-volume,
time-boxed back-office processes through SS&C Blue Prism's "digital workers" (RPA, with AI added)
across supply chain and sales/marketing. The pitch from the team lead: some processes involve large
transaction volumes in short windows where no amount of added headcount can match automation's pace.

## The numbers
- $15.3M in annual business value (vendor-reported).
- 40,600 hours of work handled by automation (vendor-reported).
- 5,000+ workdays "given back to the business" (vendor-reported).
- Process-level before/after times, headcount change, and the AI-vs-RPA split: not broken out.

## What broke / the caveats
This is an SS&C Blue Prism customer story, i.e. marketing, and it is mostly RPA ("digital workers")
with AI framing layered on; the case study does not separate what the AI actually contributed from
plain rule-based automation. The dollar and hours figures are vendor-reported with no methodology,
and "business value" is undefined (cost saved vs value created). Useful as a named, dated retail
automation deployment with a concrete scale, but the numbers should carry the vendor caveat and not
be presented as AI-specific.

## Why it matters for our beat
Retail back-office automation at a national scale, named and dated, in a traditional bricks-and-mortar
retailer. Adds sector variety (retail) to a corpus otherwise heavy on insurance and finance, and is a
clean example of the RPA-rebadged-as-AI pattern to watch for.
