# Nick's writing voice

This is the single source of truth for how nmajor.com sounds. If you are writing
anything a reader, subscriber, or customer will see, you follow this — no exceptions,
no "this is just a button." Consistency is the point: every word should feel like it
came from the same hand — Nick's.

Read `overview.md` for who this is and who we write for. The short version: nmajor.com
is Nick Major's personal brand — a named person with a real point of view on AI, writing
first-person and opinionated, and allowed to pull readers toward the consultancy (a
tasteful commercial pull, never a hype push). Readers are decision-makers (owners, CEOs,
CIOs, COOs, operators) figuring out how to actually use AI. They are busy, skeptical, and
have read enough hype for a lifetime.

This voice is *more* opinionated and more first-person than the neutral sibling orgs.
Keep the anti-hype, plain, specific, honest core below; the difference is that here Nick
takes a side, writes as himself, and can point to the work he does.

## The voice in one line

Plain, specific, and honest — the way a smart colleague explains something to you, not
the way a brochure sells to you.

## The five things that matter most

1. **Anti-grandiosity is the whole brand.** We earn trust by underclaiming, not
   overclaiming. Say what we know, name our sources, admit what is uncertain. The
   moment a sentence sounds like marketing, it has failed.
2. **Specific beats general, every time.** "Process invoices 40% faster" not "much
   faster." The named study, not "a recent study." A real example, not an adjective.
   Specifics are what make a claim believable.
3. **Write the way you'd say it out loud.** If you would not say a sentence to a
   colleague over coffee, rewrite it. This is the final test for every line.
4. **Cut the throat-clearing.** Start on the point. Delete the wind-up and the
   summary that restates what you just said.
5. **Vary the rhythm.** Mix short sentences with long ones. Some one-line paragraphs.
   AI writing has a metronome cadence; human writing does not.

## Compact and dense (the reader is a busy CEO)

Our reader is a decision-maker with no time. Posts are short, information-dense, and get to
the point fast. This is not optional.

- **Get to the point in the first line.** No wind-up, no "in this post," no setup. The first
  sentence carries a fact or the claim.
- **Short paragraphs.** Two to four sentences, often fewer. A one-line paragraph is good. A
  paragraph that fills the screen is a defect, break it up.
- **Every sentence earns its place.** If a line repeats, hedges twice, or just transitions,
  cut it. Density over completeness: state the idea, the reason, and the evidence, then move.
- **Aim short.** Most posts land well under 800 words. If it runs long, it is usually padding
  to cut, not detail to keep. Length is earned by substance, never by throat-clearing.

## Hard bans (these are absolute)

### No em dashes. Ever.
Do not use `—` anywhere, in any copy, including page-title separators. Restructure the
sentence instead. Use a period, a comma, parentheses, or a colon. Two short sentences
almost always beat one sentence held together by an em dash. (En dashes `–` in number
ranges like "8–10" are fine; the ban is the em dash as a writing device.)

### No "It's not X, it's Y" and its family.
Banned: "It's not just X, it's Y." "Not X. Y." "It isn't X, it's Y." "More than X, it's
Y." Models reach for this construction constantly to manufacture emphasis. Just state Y.

### No rule-of-three reflex.
Do not default to three-item lists and "adjective, adjective, adjective" triplets
("clear, sourced, and free of hype"). Use two, or four, or a single strong word. Vary it.

### No banned vocabulary.
The full list lives in `blacklist.md` in this folder. Read it. The worst offenders to
never use: delve, leverage, utilize, unlock, unleash, elevate, harness, empower,
robust, seamless, seamlessly, game-changer, revolutionize, transform (as hype),
supercharge, cutting-edge, navigate (figurative), landscape, realm, tapestry, journey
(figurative), testament, foster, streamline, "in today's world," "it's important to
note," "it's worth noting," "in conclusion," "moreover," "furthermore," "dive in/into,"
"embark," "at the forefront," "paving the way," "move the needle," "best-in-class."

### No fake-significance tails.
Banned: "..., highlighting its importance." "..., underscoring the shift." "..., a
testament to..." "..., reflecting a broader trend." These assert significance the
writing has not earned. End the sentence at the fact.

### No formatting tells.
- Headings in **sentence case**, not Title Case.
- Bold sparingly. Do not bold a key term in every bullet.
- No emoji as structure or in headings.
- Never ship markdown or citation artifacts (`**`, stray `#`, `:contentReference`, etc.).

## Rhythm and structure

- **Vary sentence length on purpose.** Follow a 30-word sentence with a 4-word one.
  Read it back: if every sentence is about the same length, you have the AI metronome.
  Break it.
- **Vary paragraph length too.** One-sentence paragraphs are good. Walls are not.
- **Don't run the same paragraph shape every time** (topic sentence, explanation,
  example, wrap). Mix it up.
- **Repeat a word rather than reaching for a synonym** to avoid repetition. "Synonym
  elegance" is an AI tell.
- **Use contractions and plain connectives.** "It's," "you'll," "don't." Use "but,"
  "so," and "and" instead of "however," "therefore," "additionally."

## Substance and credibility

- **Replace exaggeration with evidence.** A number, a named source, a concrete example
  the reader can check. If you can't back a claim, soften it or cut it.
- **Take a position.** One clear sentence of what Nick actually thinks beats balanced
  mush. This is a personal brand: a real opinion, in the first person, is the point.
  Generic both-sides writing reads as machine output.
- **State realistic outcomes.** Don't promise what Nick can't deliver. Honesty
  compounds; hype is a one-time withdrawal. This holds double for any commercial pull.
- **Guard against overclaiming, every time.** This is anti-grandiosity in practice. Scan for
  absolutes ("always," "never," "cannot," "guarantees," "eliminates," "any company can"),
  causal overreach ("X drives Y" when X only correlates), and rough rules of thumb stated as
  laws. Soften each to what we can defend, back it with evidence, or cut it. A heuristic is
  labeled as one ("a rough test," "usually"). When unsure how strong a claim can be, make it
  weaker. Underclaiming costs us nothing; one caught overclaim costs the credibility the
  whole piece was for.
- **First person, and address the reader as "you."** Write as Nick ("I"), and in
  reader-facing copy lean on "you/your" more than any "we/our." It's one person talking
  to one reader.
- **Plain words over big ones.** "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "About"
  not "regarding." Plain language is the antidote to grandiosity, and it is also the
  most reliable way to not sound like AI.

## Genre notes

**Homepage / landing copy.** Lead with what Nick does and why it's worth the reader's
time, in concrete terms. Benefits grounded in proof, not adjectives. Trust signals (a
named person, real numbers, sources) carry the credibility, not hype words. Short. Every
section should survive the question "would a skeptical CEO keep reading?"

**Essays and takes.** An essay is the substantive piece — it is the blog post, the
newsletter issue (Actual Intelligence, by Nicholas Major), and the source for LinkedIn
atomization, all one body of writing. A take is a short, one-line opinion. Open on the
point or a concrete detail, never "In today's world." One genuine position per piece,
stated as Nick's own. Cite sources inline and name them. Vary structure. End when you're
done, not with a summary.

**Email.** One email, one ask. The subject line is honest, not clickbait, and reads
like a person wrote it. Conversational and direct. Signed by Nick.

**Commercial pull (not push).** This is the one place in the system where pointing
toward the consultancy belongs. Keep it honest and low-key: earn trust with the writing,
then let a plain pointer ("this is the kind of thing I help companies with") do the work.
No hype, no hard sell, no urgency tricks. A pull the reader chooses to follow, never a
push. When in doubt, under-sell.

## Workflow: write, then audit

Don't one-shot it. After drafting, run three passes:

1. **Audit** — scan for em dashes, banned words (`blacklist.md`), "not X but Y,"
   rule-of-three, throat-clearing, fake-significance tails, Title Case headings, and
   overclaims (absolutes, causal overreach, heuristics stated as laws).
2. **Rhythm** — read it aloud. Mark every sentence that's the same length as its
   neighbor and every line you'd never say to a colleague. Rewrite those.
3. **Substance** — is every claim specific and backed? Is there one clear position?
   Cut anything that sounds like a brochure.
