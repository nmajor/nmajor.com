## What they did
FranklinCovey, the public leadership-training company, started replacing packaged SaaS
with low-code and AI-built tools under CIO Blaine Carter. Two builds are on the record.
A "fiscal binder" finance project replaced a roughly $50k/year software solution and the
manual circulation of a physical binder among a 10-person finance team. And an RFP
(request for proposal) workflow replaced dedicated RFP software with an AI-augmented
low-code tool. Carter's stated principle is "configuration, not customization," and that
each build starts from "partnership with the business unit feeling the pain." The primary
build environment is Make, a low-code automation platform.

## The numbers
- Fiscal binder: processing time cut from 30 days to "a few days"; ~$100k in avoided
  software spend plus ~$10k in reduced external audit fees. (CIO-reported.)
- RFP tool: 85–90% reduction in RFP completion time; $30k–$40k/year saved in software
  licenses. (CIO-reported.)
- The $50k/year figure is the price of the finance software they were willing to pay
  before building the replacement. (CIO-reported.)

## What broke / the caveats
All figures are self-reported by the CIO in an interview, not externally audited. The
piece is an enterprise-IT trade article (diginomica), which is more credible than a vendor
case study, but it is still the company describing its own wins. The article does not give
a maintenance or security accounting for the in-house tools, so the "second bill" (who
maintains these, what happens when Carter's team moves on) is not addressed in the source.

## Why it matters for our beat
A named, public, non-tech company with a named CIO going on the record about replacing
bought software with low-code/AI builds is rare and credible. The "configuration, not
customization" mantra and the "partner with the team feeling the pain" framing are a
real-world governance answer, not vendor copy. It is the strongest "saving is real" anchor
in the non-engineers-building-tools set.
