JPMorgan staff now spend more on AI tokens than some of them earn in salary. Its investor filings barely mention AI at all.

A new census from the Institute of Applied AI read every 2025 annual report from a traditional US company that mentions AI, all 2,112 of them. Insurance named AI in 82.9% of filings, more than tech itself. Banks came last, at 22.8%.

Read that fast and you'd think banks are behind. They are not. JPMorgan has 450 generative-AI projects underway and put 200,000 employees on its internal assistant in eight months. RBC's token use jumped 500% in a year. Banks are among the heaviest AI users in the economy. They just don't write it down.

The silence is rational. Almost everything a bank does with AI touches a regulated decision, like who gets a loan and at what rate. Put a model near that and every public sentence becomes a fair-lending or securities-disclosure risk. Insurers say more partly because regulators now require them to document it.

So the filings don't map who uses AI. They map who can afford to admit it. What you run is an engineering question. What you claim about it in public is a liability question, and the honest answer is sometimes less than what you have built.

Want your own real picture? Line up three things side by side: what you actually run, what you say about it in public, and what each of those claims would cost you if it turned out wrong. The gaps are where your exposure lives.

Then the question worth sitting with: if someone read only your company's public statements, would they know what you have actually deployed?
