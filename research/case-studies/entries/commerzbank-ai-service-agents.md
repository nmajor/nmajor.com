## What they did
Commerzbank, one of Germany's largest banks, put AI customer-service agents into its digital channels to handle routine inquiries autonomously and unify aging support systems, with regulatory compliance and fraud protection as explicit design constraints. Two named systems surfaced from two cloud vendors: "Ava," built on Microsoft Foundry Agent Service, and "Bene," described in Google Cloud materials. The sources do not reconcile whether these are different channels, different generations, or competing framings of overlapping work.

## The numbers
- "Ava" (Microsoft-reported): 30,000+ customer conversations per month, 75% resolved autonomously, 24/7.
- "Bene" (Google Cloud-reported): 2M+ customer chats handled, 70% of inquiries resolved.
- No cost, headcount, or satisfaction figures disclosed.

## What broke / the caveats
Both sources are vendor-marketing pages, and they don't agree with each other, which is exactly why confidence is low. The two figures ("30,000/month" vs "2M+ chats") are an order of magnitude apart and are never reconciled; they may count different things (Ava = a newer Foundry agent; Bene = a longer-running chatbot). Nothing here is independently verified. What is solid across both: a major regulated bank runs AI agents that autonomously resolve roughly 70-75% of routine customer inquiries, with compliance built in.

## Why it matters for our beat
A named, traditional, heavily-regulated European bank operating AI service agents at real scale, and a clean example of the same deployment getting two different vendor spins. Useful as a corroborating data point for "AI customer service that stuck," and as a caution on reading vendor customer-story numbers.
