ORIGINATOR: Eugene M. Schwartz, legendary direct-response copywriter. Book first
published 1966. Widely regarded as the most influential book on advertising/copywriting.

## Foundational principle (the most-quoted idea in the book)

VERBATIM (paraphrase confirmed across sources of Schwartz's central thesis):
"Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears
and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those
already existing desires onto a particular product."

Implication for the OPENING/lead: the copywriter's job is NOT to manufacture
excitement; it is to CHANNEL pre-existing mass desire. The hook works by tapping a
desire the reader already has, not by inventing one. (This is highly compatible with
an anti-hype voice — it reframes the hook as recognition/relevance, not manipulation.)

## THE 5 STAGES OF AWARENESS (where the prospect is mentally)

Schwartz says the lead/opening MUST match how aware the prospect already is. This is
the single most important determinant of how to open.

1. UNAWARE — Doesn't know they have a problem (or won't admit it). Usually not worth
   marketing to directly. Opening must use story/indirection to even raise the issue.
2. PROBLEM-AWARE — Feels the problem/pain, but doesn't know solutions exist. Open by
   naming the pain precisely (Problem-Solution lead).
3. SOLUTION-AWARE — Knows solutions exist, hasn't chosen one, doesn't know your
   product. Open by differentiating your mechanism.
4. PRODUCT-AWARE — Knows your product, not sure it's right for them. Open with proof,
   specifics, differentiation.
5. MOST-AWARE — Knows your product well, on the cusp of buying. Just needs "the deal."
   Open with the offer directly.

KEY RULE: "The more aware your prospect, the more direct your headline can be. The
less aware, the more you must lead with story, intrigue, or the problem."

## THE 5 STAGES OF MARKET SOPHISTICATION (how tired the market is of the claims)

How many times has the market heard claims like yours? Each stage requires escalating
the opening.

1. FIRST TO MARKET — No competition. Lead with the plain, direct claim ("Lose weight").
2. SECOND/COMPETITION ENTERS — Others make the same claim. Lead by enlarging/outdoing
   the claim (bigger, faster, more specific number).
3. CLAIMS EXHAUSTED / MARKET JADED — Audience numb to bigger claims. Lead with a NEW
   MECHANISM: a unique reason/process for WHY it works ("a new way to...").
4. MECHANISM ELABORATION — Competitors copy the mechanism. Lead by making YOUR
   mechanism easier, faster, more credible, removing objections.
5. MARKET FULLY SOPHISTICATED — Numb to everything. Lead with IDENTIFICATION: connect
   with WHO the reader is, emotional/identity resonance, "why" you exist.

NOTE: The "new mechanism" move (stage 3) is highly relevant to a skeptical, jaded,
expert audience — it's about offering a genuinely new explanation rather than a louder
claim. Credible-friendly IF the mechanism is real.

## THE SIX TYPES OF LEADS (opening structures)

NOTE ON ATTRIBUTION: The clean "six leads" taxonomy is codified in *Great Leads*
(2011) by Michael Masterson (Mark Ford) and John Forde, building directly on
Schwartz's awareness framework. They map each lead to an awareness level. Most-aware
markets get the most direct leads; least-aware get the most indirect.

1. THE OFFER LEAD — Direct invitation to buy/try/join. Use at MOST-AWARE.
   VERBATIM: "the reader already knows who you are, what you do, and what you have to
   sell, they just need to know 'the deal.'" Example: a "$1 deal" trial offer.

2. THE PROMISE LEAD — Lead with the biggest/best benefit or claim. Use at high
   awareness (product/solution aware). Example pattern: "[Achieve the big result] —
   [without the usual cost/effort]."

3. THE PROBLEM-SOLUTION LEAD — Open by naming the reader's pain ("hot button"), then
   pivot to the solution. Use at problem-aware to solution-aware.

4. THE SECRET LEAD — Tease hard-to-obtain knowledge, a hidden system or method. Use
   mid-range awareness. (RISK: can tip into clickbait/"one weird trick" — only
   credible if the "secret" is a real, specific, deliverable insight.)

5. THE PROCLAMATION LEAD — A bold, indirect, often surprising statement/declaration
   that disarms the reader. Use lower-to-mid awareness.

6. THE STORY LEAD — Draw the reader in through narrative; build relationship before
   the pitch. Use at UNAWARE (lowest awareness). Classic example referenced: "The Tale
   of Two Young Men" (the Wall Street Journal subscription letter by Martin Conroy —
   two men, same college, one became a manager, one became president; the difference
   was...).

## Other Schwartz "breakthrough copy" techniques relevant to openings
- CONCENTRATION: focus the prospect's desire by closing off alternative ways to satisfy it.
- GRADUALIZATION: get the prospect agreeing/believing in small steps BEFORE you state
  your big claim — so the claim lands on a primed, agreeing mind.

## ANTI-HYPE ASSESSMENT
- TRANSFERS WELL: awareness matching (meet readers where they are), sophistication's
  "new mechanism" move (give a real new explanation, not a louder claim),
  problem-solution lead, story lead, the channel-don't-manufacture-desire thesis,
  gradualization (earn agreement step by step).
- HANDLE WITH CARE: the Secret Lead and Promise Lead — fine if the secret is a genuine
  specific insight and the promise is literally true and specific; dangerous if used
  as a tease/overclaim.
