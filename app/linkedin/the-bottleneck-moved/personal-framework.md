Most AI rollouts that fail have the same problem, and a better model won't fix it: they point the tool at the wrong step.

AI is fast at producing work. The slow, expensive step is a person checking that output and signing off on it, and that is where your real constraint sits now. Speed up production and you just pile more work in front of the person who has to own it.

Allianz's UK arm gave its underwriters an AI assistant and saved about 135 days of document-hunting in a year. It worked because the assistant did the grunt work in front of the decision. The underwriter still owns the call.

Here is what the companies actually getting returns do differently:

1. Fix the process before you add the tool. Bolt AI onto a workflow that was already a mess and you get the mess, faster.

2. Point AI at the grunt work in front of the decision, not at the decision itself.

3. Pick tasks where a wrong answer is cheap to catch. That is what makes it safe to move fast.

4. Protect the person who signs off. Their capacity to judge is the ceiling on your throughput, so don't bury them.

The pattern across the big consulting surveys is boring and consistent: the returns come from redesigning the work around the tool, not from buying a better model or cutting the people who own the output.
