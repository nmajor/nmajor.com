For twenty years, build versus buy had one sensible answer: buy. AI just broke that math.

A Washington school district expects to save $250,000 building the tools it used to buy. None of its staff are coders.

For most of two decades, building your own software was a money pit of cost, delay, and upkeep, worth it only when what you needed was genuinely one of a kind. So companies rented, and the rent went up every year. The two costs that made building irrational, the money and the wait, have fallen close to zero, even for people who have never written a line of code.

It is not one district. FranklinCovey's CIO, Blaine Carter, says deciding what to buy was "the least controversial question in enterprise IT" for most of his career, and he is now replacing packaged software with tools his own team builds. In the same Retool survey, more than a third of teams said they had already replaced a paid tool with one they built, and nearly four in five planned to build more this year. A construction firm in Spain reports building its own project and operations system, web and mobile both, without hiring a single developer.

If you sign the software checks for a real-world business, this is worth some math. Some of what you rent, you could now own.

Which of the tools you rent could you actually build this year?
