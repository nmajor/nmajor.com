# GDPR Article 22 — Automated individual decision-making

**Citation:** Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (General Data Protection Regulation), Article 22,
"Automated individual decision-making, including profiling."

**Sources:**
- https://gdpr-info.eu/art-22-gdpr/
- https://gdpr-text.com/read/article-22/
- ICO guidance: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/individual-rights/individual-rights/rights-related-to-automated-decision-making-including-profiling/

## Verbatim text

**Art. 22(1):** "The data subject shall have the right not to be subject to a decision
based solely on automated processing, including profiling, which produces legal effects
concerning him or her or similarly significantly affects him or her."

**Art. 22(2):** The right does not apply where the decision (a) is necessary for entering
into or performing a contract; (b) is authorised by Union or Member State law with suitable
safeguards; or (c) is based on the data subject's explicit consent.

**Art. 22(3):** "In the cases referred to in points (a) and (c) of paragraph 2, the data
controller shall implement suitable measures to safeguard the data subject's rights and
freedoms and legitimate interests, **at least the right to obtain human intervention on the
part of the controller, to express his or her point of view and to contest the decision.**"

**Art. 22(4):** Such decisions shall not be based on special categories of personal data
(Art. 9(1)) unless Art. 9(2)(a) or (g) applies with suitable safeguards.

## Mapping to Nick's thesis (STRONG as a legal-philosophical instantiation)

This is the law encoding *exactly* Nick's intuition: a person has, as a default right, the
right **not to be subject to a solely automated decision** with legal or similarly
significant effect — and, where such decisions are allowed, an irreducible right to **human
intervention, to voice their side, and to contest** the outcome. Accuracy is nowhere in the
test; the safeguard is *human answerability and contestability*, not correctness.

**Two honest caveats for citation:**
1. GDPR is EU law; the nH Predict / Lokken case is US (no direct US federal equivalent).
   Use Art. 22 as a statement of a *principle a major legal regime has already adopted*, not
   as the governing law of the case.
2. Wachter, Mittelstadt & Floridi (2017) argue the Article is weaker in practice than often
   claimed (a "right to be informed," not a robust "right to explanation") — see raw file
   06. Cite Art. 22 for the *principle it enshrines*, and flag the scholarly debate over its
   teeth rather than overclaiming.
