## What they did
Peninsula School District, a public K-12 district in Gig Harbor, Washington, has
administrators, most without any computer-science background, building their own software
with Claude Code instead of renewing ed-tech subscriptions. They use $200/month Claude Code
seats plus an internal "AI Studio" (~$600/month) that runs on data-privacy agreements with
Google Gemini, OpenAI, and Anthropic. Tools built so far include LessonLens, which gives
teachers coaching feedback on uploaded lesson recordings, and a CTE purchasing-request
dashboard in development.

## The numbers
- Before: paying for "three or four" subscription tools it now plans to drop, including
  Informed K12 (HR/finance workflow automation).
- After: expects to cancel those contracts for the 2026-27 school year.
- Cost / spend: projected saving of roughly $200k-$250k/year (EdWeek frames it ~$200k,
  K12 Dive up to $250k). Tooling cost is $200/month per admin plus ~$600/month for AI Studio.
- Time / headcount / volume: several administrators building part-time; no new hires reported.

## What broke / the caveats
The savings are a projection for next school year, not realized yet, so treat the
$200k-$250k as expected, not banked. Trade press (K12 Dive, EdWeek) is the primary source
here; the content-farm rewrites (bahrku.com, theaicronicle.com) that also surfaced this are
not. CIO Kris Hagel names the core risk himself: "You can't just throw up any old thing
that AI writes for you... there's got to be some vetting going on behind the scenes." Both
outlets flag technical debt from unvetted AI-generated code and the difficulty of proving ROI.

## Why it matters for our beat
A traditional, budget-constrained public-sector body, not a tech startup, having
non-technical staff replace paid software is one of the strongest "ordinary organization
puts AI into real operations" stories in this cluster, and the named CIO caveats keep it honest.
