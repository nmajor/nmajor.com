# NYT Customer Insight Group, "The Psychology of Sharing" (2011)

Sources:
- NYT press release (primary): https://investors.nytco.com/news-and-events/press-releases/news-details/2011/The-New-York-Times-Completes-Research-on-Psychology-of-Sharing/default.aspx
- EveryoneSocial synthesis: https://everyonesocial.com/blog/the-psychology-of-how-and-why-we-share
- Column Five synthesis: https://www.columnfivemedia.com/psychology-behind-share-creative-content

Study: The New York Times Customer Insight Group, in collaboration with Latitude
Research. Two qualitative phases + a quantitative online survey of 2,500+
medium-to-heavy online content sharers. First major study of WHY people share (vs.
how). This is the key source on the psychology of RESHARING / broadcasting to one's
own audience (distinct from merely engaging).

## The five core motivations to share (verbatim/near-verbatim)
1. **To bring valuable and entertaining content to others.** 94% "carefully consider
   how the information they share will be useful to the recipient." (People share to
   inform/amuse/help — the practical-value motive.)
2. **To define ourselves to others.** **68% share to give people a better sense of
   who they are and what they care about.** "We all have our own personal brand, and
   the content that we share reflects our identities." Sharing is a performative act
   of self-definition: "This is me."
3. **To grow and nourish relationships.** 73% share to connect with others who share
   their interests; 78% share because it lets them stay in touch with people they
   otherwise wouldn't.
4. **Self-fulfillment.** People share because they enjoy it and feel more involved/
   connected. "People enjoy content MORE when they share it — and when it is shared
   with them."
5. **To get the word out about causes or brands** they believe in / want to support.

## The six sharer personas (from the same study)
Altruists (helpful, connection-driven), Careerists (share to build professional
capital / network — highly relevant to LinkedIn), Hipsters (identity/cutting-edge,
build identity through sharing), Boomerangs (seek reaction/validation), Connectors
(relationship-driven planners), Selectives (share resourceful, targeted info to
specific recipients).

## The load-bearing insight for LinkedIn breakout
Resharing is overwhelmingly an act of **identity signaling and social currency**:
68% share to signal who they are and what they care about; sharing content is how
people manage their own personal brand. On LinkedIn specifically, the dominant
persona is the **Careerist** — a professional reshares/reacts because it makes THEM
look informed, credible, and on top of their field to their own audience.

→ Therefore a post breaks out when resharing/commenting on it lets the resharer say
something about themselves. "Look — this is the AI risk I've been warning my board
about" is a status-positive, identity-defining reshare. A niche checklist ("5
questions to ask before you approve an AI tool") is useful to DO privately (a save),
but resharing it says little flattering about the resharer's identity, so it does
not trigger the broadcast motive. This is the psychological bridge between STEPPS
"social currency" (file 02) and the engagement-vs-breakout distinction: **saves are
private utility; reshares/broad comments are public identity.**
