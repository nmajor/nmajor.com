A US bank just filed an SEC disclosure because an employee used the wrong AI tool.

No hacker. No breach. Just someone trying to move a little faster.

To get through the work, the employee ran customer records through an AI app the bank hadn't approved. Names, Social Security numbers, dates of birth. Because of how sensitive that is, it became a material event, the kind public companies have to report to the SEC. CB Financial, a Pennsylvania bank, disclosed it in an 8-K filing this year. As far as anyone can tell, it is the first SEC filing ever caused by an employee's AI shortcut.

Here is the part that stuck with me.

The job almost certainly did not need those Social Security numbers at all. X them out, and the tool could have helped just the same. The breach-worthy data was never required to get the value. It got pasted in because pasting everything was easier than pasting the part that mattered.

That is the quiet lesson under the headline. The access your people hand an AI tool and the access the task actually needs are rarely the same thing. A lot of the risk you are worried about, you can simply leave out. Blacking out a name or a number takes seconds. It is the cheapest security control you own.

So the first question before anyone runs real data through an AI tool is not "is this tool safe." It is "does it even need this data, or would a redacted version do just as well?"

Usually, it would.

If your team is using AI on real customer data: who decided what it was allowed to see?
