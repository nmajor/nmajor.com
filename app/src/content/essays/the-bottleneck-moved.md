More than [1,600 court filings](https://www.damiencharlotin.com/hallucinations/) have been caught citing cases the AI invented, and the lawyers who filed them are getting fined and thrown off their cases. The tool wrote each brief in seconds. Checking it and signing it was still the lawyer's job, and that part never got faster.

That pattern explains most of the disappointing AI numbers, and I think it should change where you point the tool. AI made producing work close to free. A blank page becomes a draft in seconds; a stack of data becomes a first-pass answer. But checking that output and putting your name on it didn't get cheaper. In most companies it got heavier, because now there's more to check. The expensive step in your operation quietly moved from making the work to owning it.

## The check is where the time goes

Watch what happens when someone actually times this. Tested on their own code, [experienced developers came out 19% slower with AI](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) while being sure they were about 20% faster. It's one study, and I'd be careful reading too much into it: these were experts on code they knew cold, which may be where AI helps least, and the real story is surely more tangled than any single cause. But part of what the stopwatch caught travels to every other job. Producing the work got quicker, and checking it got slower, because there was now more to check.

And when the check gets skimped, the cost lands on whoever's downstream. Researchers gave that a name, [workslop](https://hbr.org/2025/09/ai-generated-workslop-is-destroying-productivity): AI output that looks finished and lacks substance. About 40% of workers report getting handed it, at roughly two hours each to sort out. The drafting was cheap. The checking bill just got mailed to a colleague.

Multiply that across a company and you get the flat headlines. Bain looked at 951 companies and found [most that tracked it got single-digit cost savings against double-digit targets](https://www.bain.com/insights/your-ai-budget-is-growing-your-returns-arent-heres-why/).

None of this is new, either. Back in 1983, Lisanne Bainbridge described the [ironies of automation](https://humanfactors101.com/2020/05/24/the-ironies-of-automation/): automate the easy parts of a job and you leave the person with the hard part, judging whether the machine got it right. And the hard part concentrates on fewer people.

## This is your operation too

The same shape holds far from software, anywhere a decision carries weight. In one real hospital deployment, a radiology AI put a [median of 239 alerts in front of each radiologist](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.13379v1), and the alert almost always arrived after the doctor had already opened the case. More flags, same number of people who can sign a diagnosis. An AI can draft the underwriting call or the loan decision too, but someone still has to own it, and that capacity is the ceiling, not the drafting speed.

There is one way to make AI look fast: stop checking its work. That's the move behind those 1,600 sanctioned briefs, and it tempts because sign-off is the only slow step left. But the checking is the quality. Skip it and all you've sped up is the production of work nobody stands behind, which costs more downstream than it ever saved.

## What the companies with returns did

They did something less exciting than buying a better model. McKinsey's surveys keep finding the strongest predictor of AI reaching the bottom line is whether the company [redesigned the workflow](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) around the tool instead of bolting it onto the old one. [The process has to come before the tool](/writing/fix-the-process-first/): put AI on top of a workflow that was already a mess and you get the mess, faster. And when Gartner studied 350 companies, the ones chasing returns purely through headcount cuts got [limited returns; the value came from making people more productive](https://fortune.com/2026/05/11/ai-automation-layoffs-gartner-study-roi/).

The boring version works. Allianz's UK arm gave its underwriters an assistant trained on the company's own manuals, and it [saved about 135 working days of document-hunting in a year](https://www.allianz.co.uk/news-and-insight/news/brian-the-ai-brain.html). It's aimed at one narrow task where a wrong answer is cheap to catch, and the underwriter still owns the call. They pointed AI at the work in front of the decision and left the decision alone.

So before you write off an AI project, look at where the finished-looking work is piling up. If you sped up production and left the person who signs off just as buried, you have a faster machine feeding the same jam.

The way out runs through that person. Point AI at the grunt work in front of them, and protect their room to do the one thing a model can't: judge whether the work is right and put their name on it. Drafting got cheap. The sign-off is the job now.
