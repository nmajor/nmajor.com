## Source: youvisible.substack.com — Authority Signals List (10 Credibility Strategies)
URL: https://youvisible.substack.com/p/authority-signals-list
Core principle quoted: **"Authority is not something you demand (it is something you display)."**
The 10 signals:
1. The Specific Niche — narrow expertise rather than broad topics
2. Quantified Results — metrics instead of vague descriptions
3. The "Pre-Mortem" Framework — discuss failure points and risk prevention
4. The Name Drop (of Ideas) — reference industry frameworks/methodologies
5. Public Correction or Refinement — nuanced takes on popular ideas
6. Consistent Cadence of Ideas — regular visibility (3-4x weekly)
7. The Source Material Link — embed links to your own resources/templates
8. The Title Rewrite — transform generic titles into value props
9. The Thoughtful Question — insightful questions challenging assumptions
10. The Specific Toolstack — mention specialized tools you actually master
Note: signals most trustworthy when they demonstrate PROOF (quantified results, specific tools, source materials) rather than claims. Weakest signals appear performative if no genuine expertise behind them.

## Source: theconversation.com — Five ways to tell if someone is a real expert (by an actual expert)
URL: https://theconversation.com/five-ways-to-tell-if-someone-is-an-expert-or-just-confident-from-an-actual-expert-188244
1. How likely is it that this person is an expert? Assess background, motivation, skills. "Experts differ from novices in memory as well as perception and categorisation of complex facts." Check if proclaimed expertise aligns with what's known about them.
2. How do they communicate in general? True experts listen more than they dominate. "an attentive style – one that prioritises listening over talking – can lead to better collaboration."
3. Do they go into depth? Genuine experts elaborate; without real knowledge people "have to stay on a superficial level" and repeat general messages.
4. Is anybody actually certain about the topic? Real experts acknowledge uncertainty using "maybe"/"could". Key distinction: "I am uncertain" vs "It is uncertain" — experts know what CAN be known; non-experts simply lack facts.
5. Can they provide info flexibly? Authentic experts adjust to audience, use terminology with ease, unlike "automatic systems" repeating identical phrases.

Build in Public — PROS:
- "Transparency can make a company feel more human and credible... people often trusting builders who are willing to show their work."
- "Transparency-fueled audience growth can lead to a quickened position of industry authority."
- "if you're the most public person in your niche, the niche will be immediately associated with you."
- Direct feedback from customers before release is priceless.
- "People remember stories more than huge numbers because stories foster connection and trust."

Build in Public — CONS:
- Sharing too much too early exposes strategic thinking to competitors.
- Pressure: "when progress is slow or messy, founders may feel compelled to project consistency or confidence that doesn't match reality." (RELEVANT to honesty tension)
- If idea is buildable over a weekend / relies on a specific positioning, may be better to keep details to team.

Polished vs Unpolished:
- Unpolished content usually does NOT harm premium positioning. "Trust is rarely created by claims alone; it is created by congruence."
- "Content credibility signals... often matter more than production quality. Specificity with concrete examples, firsthand experience from direct work, and nuance that acknowledges trade-offs all increase trust."

Middle ground: "The question isn't whether or not to build in public, but how much" — find the sweet spot maximizing benefits while minimizing risk.
