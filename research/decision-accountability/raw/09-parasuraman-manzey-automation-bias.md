# Complacency and Bias in Human Use of Automation — Parasuraman & Manzey (2010)

**Citation:** Raja Parasuraman & Dietrich H. Manzey, "Complacency and Bias in Human Use of
Automation: An Attentional Integration," *Human Factors: The Journal of the Human Factors
and Ergonomics Society*, Vol. 52, No. 3 (June 2010), pp. 381–410.
DOI: 10.1177/0018720810376055

**Source:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0018720810376055

## The idea

Reviews and integrates the empirical literature on two phenomena that undermine the "human
in the loop":
- **Automation complacency** — under multitask load, operators under-monitor an automated
  aid, over-trusting it. Occurs in **both novices and experts**, and **cannot be overcome by
  simple practice**.
- **Automation bias** — operators over-rely on automated outputs, producing both *omission*
  errors (missing events the automation didn't flag) and *commission* errors (following an
  automated recommendation even against contradictory information). Occurs in novices and
  experts and **cannot be prevented by training or instructions.**

Both are unified as attentional effects: attention allocation away from the raw evidence and
toward the automated recommendation.

## Mapping to Nick's thesis (STRONG for the "why the human safeguard fails in practice" leg)

This is the empirical mechanism behind Elish's moral crumple zone. It explains *why*
"a human reviews every denial" is not a real safeguard: humans systematically defer to the
machine's output (automation bias), especially under caseload pressure (complacency under
multitask load), and — critically — this is **not fixable by training**. So the promised
"human check" degrades into a rubber stamp by predictable cognitive mechanism, not by
individual laziness. Pair Parasuraman & Manzey (the *why*, empirically) with Elish (the
*consequence*: the human absorbs the blame anyway).
