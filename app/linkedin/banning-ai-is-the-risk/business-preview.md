This week's issue: a Pennsylvania bank employee ran customer data through an unapproved AI tool to move faster. It became the first SEC 8-K filing ever caused by an employee's AI shortcut.

The easy lesson is "keep data out of AI." It is the wrong one. AI is only useful when you give it something real to work with. The better move is older than AI: give it the least access the job needs, and contain the rest.

For most companies, who buy their tools rather than build them, the practical version is short:

- Put people on the company's paid AI account, not their personal ones.
- Pick a tool whose terms promise, in writing, not to train on your data.
- Set one rule everyone follows: customer records never go into a personal account.

That last rule alone would have spared the bank its filing.

The full piece covers least privilege, why prompt injection makes AI different from any tool you have secured before, and the five questions to ask before you let an AI near anything that matters.
