# The Responsibility Gap — Andreas Matthias (2004)

**Citation:** Andreas Matthias, "The responsibility gap: Ascribing responsibility for the actions of learning automata," *Ethics and Information Technology*, Vol. 6, No. 3 (2004), pp. 175–183. DOI: 10.1007/s10676-004-3422-1

**Sources:**

## Core claim (from publisher abstract / search synthesis)

Traditionally the manufacturer/operator of a machine is held morally and legally
responsible for its operation. But autonomous, *learning* machines (based on neural
networks, genetic algorithms, agent architectures) create a new situation: the
machine's behavior is determined during operation by what it learns, so the
manufacturer/operator **in principle cannot predict future machine behavior** and
therefore cannot be fairly held morally responsible or liable for it.

Matthias frames the resulting dilemma sharply: **either** society refuses to use this
class of machine, **or** it accepts a **"responsibility gap" that cannot be bridged by
traditional concepts of responsibility.** No one — not the programmer, not the operator,
not the machine — can be fairly held responsible for the outcome.

## Key extracted phrasing

- "There is an increasing class of machine actions, where the traditional ways of
  responsibility ascription are not compatible with our sense of justice and the moral
  framework of society because nobody has enough control over the machine's actions to be
  able to assume responsibility for them." (widely quoted formulation of the gap)
- The choice is stated as a genuine societal decision: not use these machines, or live
  with an unbridgeable gap.

## Mapping to Nick's thesis (STRONG)

This is the philosophical spine of the "accountability can't be automated" argument.
Nick's move — *there may be a class of decision we should refuse to hand to a machine
at all* — is literally Matthias's first horn of the dilemma ("not to use this kind of
machine"). Note one honest caveat for citation: Matthias's paper is specifically about
*learning* automata whose behavior is unpredictable. nH Predict is a predictive model,
arguably less "autonomous/learning" in Matthias's strict sense — but the gap he
identifies (control decoupled from responsibility) generalizes cleanly, and later
literature (Santoni de Sio, Elish) applies it to exactly this kind of system.
