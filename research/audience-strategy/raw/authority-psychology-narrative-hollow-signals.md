## Source: WebSearch "halo effect competence signaling expertise perception psychology"
Links:
- https://www.betterup.com/blog/halo-effect
- https://web.colby.edu/cogblog/2018/04/26/that-perfect-person-isnt-quite-so-perfect/
- https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8024691/
- arxiv 2507.05046 (trust in ChatGPT mixed-methods)
Key points:
- Halo effect = attribution bias; one noticeable trait colors perception of overall competence/character. First identified by Edward Thorndike — generalizing from a single positive characteristic to overly favorable overall evaluation.
- "Users who consistently post high-quality content are often perceived as more competent in their field, regardless of their actual expertise." (Caution: perception-reality gap.)
- Halo effect causes trust transfer: "we trust those who have expertise in one area in areas irrelevant to their actual knowledge base." <-- DIRECTLY relevant: Nick's engineering expertise can create a halo into "AI consulting." Honest use = leverage genuine adjacency; dishonest = let halo imply expertise he lacks.
- First impressions strongly influence long-term perception even when new evidence emerges; "early success or visibility can protect individuals from later scrutiny, and strong communication skills may overshadow gaps in subject-matter expertise or execution." (This is the RISK and the lever — overshadowing gaps is the ethically dangerous part.)
- Perceived expertise has moderate positive association with predictability — competence signals reinforce expectations of consistent performance.

## Source: WebSearch "vanity metrics / hollow credibility signals — what doesn't build trust"
Links:
- https://www.trustsignals.com/blog/the-trust-signals-that-influence-ai-recommendations...
- https://www.wsiworld.com/blog/authority-is-the-new-currency-in-ai-search-5-signals-that-establish-trust
- https://solutionsreview.com/marketing-automation/what-is-the-vanity-trap-in-pr...
- https://www.semrush.com/blog/ai-search-trust-signals/
HOLLOW signals (vanity metrics): likes, follower counts, ad impressions — "look impressive but may not reflect true business impact."
- Critical distinction: AWARENESS metrics (how many encountered your brand) vs TRUST signals (how many independent, authoritative sources have vouched for you).
- "A company can accumulate 50,000 LinkedIn followers without a single independent authority having written about it, reviewed it, or recognized it in any substantive way."
REAL trust signals: independent/external validation — "AI does not rely on what you say about yourself—it looks at who else is validating your expertise." Backlinks/brand mentions from authoritative sites = third-party proof. Relationships with niche publications, blogs, analysts, creators, expert voices.

## Source: WebSearch "fake it till you make it — ethics / honest alternative"
Links:
- https://technologyandsociety.org/on-the-morality-of-fake-it-till-you-make-it-in-high-tech/ (IEEE Technology and Society)
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0007681321002068 (hazards of cultural norm in entrepreneurship)
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fake_it_till_you_make_it
- https://www.mergesociety.com/startup-stories/fake-it
THE LINE (clearest statements):
- "'Faking' credentials, certifications, or specific experience crosses the ethical line."
- Silicon Valley version "encourages entrepreneurs to deceptively claim their new ventures already exhibit successful characteristics... an unorthodox tactic that goes against truthful and honest conduct."
- "perpetuates the dangerous notion that value is tied to perception rather than reality."
- Grey area framed as: "not whether you are confident or capable. It is about commitment, failure, and risk. There's a danger of taking it too far and lying about your level of knowledge, status, experience or skills."
- Ethical alternative: "demonstrating real competence through work and commitment rather than projecting false confidence." Genuine skill "cannot be masked by mere appearances."
SUMMARY OF LINE: Confidence/optimism/posture about ABILITY TO DELIVER = acceptable. Fabricating credentials, certifications, track record, years of experience, client count = fraud.

## Source: WebSearch "specificity, citing sources, admitting uncertainty, original analysis build credibility"
Links:
- https://courses.lumenlearning.com/wm-writingskillslab/chapter/building-credibility-through-source-integration/
- arxiv 2410.21360 (textual credibility signals survey, LLM era)
- https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11336192/ (hedging in medical discourse)
- arxiv 2304.01789 (communication of statistics/uncertainty)
Key points:
- "A well-cited source shows that the author is building on existing research rather than making unsupported claims." Signal phrases highlight the source's reputation/qualifications -> adds credibility. Explicit references increase credibility perception and "show your work as a researcher."
- Admitting uncertainty does NOT undermine credibility: "empirical evidence suggests that clear communication of numeric uncertainty does not undermine trust in communicators or scientists." (Counters the fear that admitting newness/uncertainty kills authority.)
- Original analysis: credibility from thorough, original evaluation of evidence/arguments/conclusions, not restating.
