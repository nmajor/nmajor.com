"We keep a human in the loop" is the most oversold phrase in corporate AI right now.

It sounds like a safeguard. Often it's a formality.

Put a person in front of the AI's output and you can tell yourself a human decides. But look at what that person is measured on. If they're rewarded for agreeing with the model, and second-guessed every time they don't, the model is deciding. The person is just signing.

Cigna showed how far that can go. Its medical directors denied more than 300,000 claims in two months, an average of 1.2 seconds each, signed off in batches without anyone opening the patient files. One doctor called it click and submit, fifty at a time. That's a signature, dressed up as a decision.

A human in the loop only counts if that human is genuinely free to say the machine is wrong, and nothing bad happens to them when they do. If overruling the AI is a career risk, the check is fake. What you have built is a rubber stamp with a name on it.

But double-checking an AI is boring, tedious work. It's a strange new problem these automated systems create. Maybe it's even a good design challenge: make the review compelling enough that people actually do it.
