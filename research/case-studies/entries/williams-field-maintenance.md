## What they did
Williams, a US natural-gas infrastructure operator, put approved AI tools (Microsoft Copilot
among them) into two places: field-crew document prep at compressor stations, and internal
software delivery. At a Virginia compressor station, a preventive-maintenance checklist for
electrical equipment that used to eat a full day was assembled in about 90 minutes using AI tools
and refined prompts. Separately, AI-assisted coding sped up application modernization, and
unifying real-time operational and commercial data cut rework in gas-storage trading.

## The numbers
- Field maintenance checklist: a full day (sometimes two) down to ~1.5 hours (company-reported).
- Software delivery: 55% lower implementation cost, 60% faster delivery (company-reported).
- Commercial: $7M uplift in "park and loan" gas-storage transactions (company-reported).
- Cost of the AI tooling, scale of rollout, number of stations: unknown.

## What broke / the caveats
This is Williams' own newsroom post, so every figure is first-party and unaudited, and presented
as narrative rather than with methodology. The maintenance win is a single station and a single
checklist type, not a fleet-wide measurement. The "$7M uplift" blends a data-integration project
with AI and is not cleanly attributable to AI alone. No mention of error rates, rework from bad AI
output, or what the crews had to check by hand. Governance language ("employees remain in control")
is reassuring but unspecific.

## Why it matters for our beat
A pipeline operator, about as traditional and safety-bound as it gets, using off-the-shelf Copilot
for field paperwork rather than a moonshot. The signal is mundane and repeatable: AI compressing
document-assembly time in heavy industry, told first-party with named people on the record.
