# What Money Can't Buy: The Moral Limits of Markets — Michael Sandel (2012)

**Citation:** Michael J. Sandel, *What Money Can't Buy: The Moral Limits of Markets*
(Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2012).

**Sources:**
- https://scholar.harvard.edu/sandel/publications/what-money-cant-buy-moral-limits-markets
- Full text PDF: http://digamo.free.fr/sandel12.pdf

## Core argument

Two objections to letting market logic into every domain:
1. **Inequality** — when money can buy everything, having it matters more.
2. **Corruption** — "market norms crowd out valuable nonmarket behavior." Putting a price on
   certain goods **corrupts** them, because "markets don't only allocate goods; they also
   express and promote certain attitudes toward the goods being exchanged." Some moral and
   civic goods are *degraded* when bought and sold.

His signature illustration: an Israeli day-care center introduced fines for late pickup and
lateness *increased* — the fine reframed a moral obligation as a purchasable fee, corrupting
the norm. The wrong *logic* applied to a domain changes and degrades it.

## Mapping to Nick's thesis (STRETCH — include only as a loose analogy, clearly flagged)

The transferable structure is real and useful: **"the moral limits of automation," by
analogy to "the moral limits of markets."** Just as some goods are corrupted by market logic
(the wrong valuation frame), some decisions may be corrupted by *automation logic* — an
actuarial/predictive frame applied where individual judgment and answerability are
constitutive of doing it *right*. Applying statistical-optimization logic to "does this
specific frail person still need care" changes the act into something else.

Honest flag: Sandel's book is about *markets/commodification*, not automation, and he does
not make the automation argument. This is Nick borrowing the *shape* of Sandel's argument,
not citing Sandel as authority on AI. Present it explicitly as an analogy ("Sandel argued X
about markets; the same structure applies to automation"), never as if Sandel wrote about
algorithms. Used honestly, it is a strong rhetorical frame; used as authority, it is a
