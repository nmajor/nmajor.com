# Subject Line Statistics — Axios HQ + FinancesOnline aggregation

## SOURCE 1: Axios HQ Research (Smart Brevity)
Source: https://www.axioshq.com/research/effective-subject-lines
Accessed: 2026-06-19
- Based on analysis of 69,000+ internal emails, 700+ organizations, June 2022 - August 2024.
- Tip: Keep subject lines to 3-6 words (31-49 characters). Optimizes for mobile.
- Tip: Avoid question marks. "the absence of a question mark, though, did make a statistical difference" in open rates. (NOTE: contradicts generic advice that questions boost opens — Axios's internal/credible audience data says no question mark performed better.)
- 70% of employees prefer leaders send vital updates via email.
- Full report adds tips on length by audience, punctuation, parts of speech, capitalization, emoji, sentiment, plus guidance from Jen Psaki.

## SOURCE 2: FinancesOnline aggregated statistics
Source: https://financesonline.com/email-subject-line-statistics/
Accessed: 2026-06-19
- "47% of email recipients decide to open emails based on the subject lines alone" (Tidio)
- "69% of email recipients report emails as spam after reading the subject line" (Invesp, 2021)
- "50% – the increase in open rates of emails with personalized subject lines" (Marketing Dive)
- "Emails with the recipient's name on the subject line have an 18.30% open rate" vs "15.70%" without (Invesp, 2021)
- "Emails with 6 to 10 words in their subject lines have the highest open rate at 21%" (Invesp, 2021)
- "40 characters are the recommended length for subject lines" (Tidio, 2020)
- "Including a sense of urgency and exclusivity in subject lines give emails a 22% higher open rate" (Invesp, 2021)
- "Companies that use emojis in subject lines have 56% higher open rates" (OptinMonster, 2020)
- "Businesses should only use one emoji at a time" (Mailchimp, 2021)
- "Malicious emails contain the word 'invoice' in the subject field 13.2% of the time" (Statista, 2020)

## ANALYST NOTE
Axios HQ is the most brand-aligned data source: Smart Brevity, plain words, short, NO question marks for serious/professional audiences. Its "avoid question marks" finding is notable because it cuts against the generic "questions boost opens" listicle advice — and aligns with a skeptical-operator audience. "Urgency/exclusivity = +22%" is real but must be used honestly (genuine deadlines only), not manufactured.
