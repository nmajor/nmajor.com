A lawyer trusted AI to write the brief. It invented the case law. That mistake has now shown up in more than 1,600 filings.

The AI wrote it in seconds. Checking it and putting a name on it was still the lawyer's job, and that part never got faster.

Lawyers just happen to get caught in public. The same shift is running through every office: AI made producing work close to free. It drafts the claim summary or the board report in seconds.

But someone still has to check that output and answer for it. AI just means there's more of it to check. The expensive step was always the sign-off, and AI didn't touch it.

So the bottleneck in your operation didn't disappear when you added AI. It moved to whoever owns the result.

Before you write off an AI project, look at where the finished-looking work is piling up. If you sped up production and left the person who signs off just as buried, you built a faster machine feeding the same jam.

Where is the finished-looking work piling up fastest in your shop, in front of the person who has to sign their name to it?
