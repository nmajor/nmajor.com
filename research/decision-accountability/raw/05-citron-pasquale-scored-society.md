# The Scored Society: Due Process for Automated Predictions — Citron & Pasquale (2014)

**Citation:** Danielle Keats Citron & Frank A. Pasquale, "The Scored Society: Due Process
for Automated Predictions," *Washington Law Review*, Vol. 89, No. 1 (2014), pp. 1–33.

**Sources:**
- https://ssrn.com/abstract=2376209
- https://digitalcommons.law.uw.edu/wlr/vol89/iss1/2/

## Core argument

Predictive scoring systems decide access to consequential goods (credit, jobs, housing,
insurance) but are **opaque and lack oversight**. The authors argue that the American
**due process tradition** should supply basic safeguards: (1) regulators must be able to
**audit/test** scoring systems for fairness and accuracy, and (2) individuals must have a
**meaningful opportunity to challenge adverse decisions** that miscategorize them. They
call this **"technological due process"** (a term from Citron's earlier 2008 work,
"Technological Due Process," *Washington University Law Review* 85:1249).

## Key extracted points

- "Though automated scoring is pervasive and consequential, it is also opaque and lacking
  oversight."
- Procedural regularity is essential for those "stigmatized" by automated scoring.
- Individuals should be "granted meaningful opportunities to challenge adverse decisions
  based on scores miscategorizing them."

## Mapping to Nick's thesis (STRONG for the "contestability/due process" leg)

This is the cleanest US-grounded scholarly source for the fourth argument: a decision can be
*illegitimate* — regardless of accuracy — if it is opaque and the subject cannot contest it.
nH Predict's alleged design (a ~90% appeal-reversal rate, counting on people not appealing)
is a textbook due-process failure in Citron & Pasquale's frame. Pair with GDPR Art. 22 (the
statutory version of the same intuition) and note the shared spine: legitimacy comes from
contestability and a human who answers, not from the model being right.
