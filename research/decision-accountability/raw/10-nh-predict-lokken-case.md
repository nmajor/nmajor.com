# The live example — nH Predict / Estate of Lokken v. UnitedHealth

**Case:** *Estate of Gene B. Lokken, et al. v. UnitedHealth Group, Inc., et al.*,
U.S. District Court, District of Minnesota (filed Nov. 2023). Defendants include
UnitedHealth Group and subsidiary NaviHealth (maker of the nH Predict tool).

**Sources:**
- https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/alerts/federal-court-orders-broad-discovery-against-uhc-ai-coverage-denial-lawsuit
- https://www.medicaleconomics.com/view/unitedhealthcare-used-ai-to-deny-patients-health-insurance-coverage-lawsuit-says
- https://www.tresslerllp.com/thought-leadership/estate-of-gene-b-lokken-et-al-v-unitedhealth-group-inc-ai-risks-in-medical-insurance-coverage-disputes/

## The facts as alleged (allegations — case ongoing)

- **The tool:** nH Predict is a **predictive algorithm (not an LLM)**. It allegedly makes
  post-acute care determinations by **matching a patient to a database of "similar"
  patients** to predict how much rehabilitation/nursing care they "should" need — rather
  than deferring to the treating physician's medical judgment.
- **Whom it hit:** elderly Medicare Advantage beneficiaries needing post-acute care
  (skilled nursing, rehab) after hospitalization.
- **Lead plaintiff:** Gene B. Lokken, 91, of Wisconsin; fractured leg and ankle in a May
  2022 fall; transferred to post-acute rehab; coverage cut off on the algorithm's
  determination while he still needed care.
- **The alleged "gotcha":** nH Predict allegedly carried an **error rate of ~90%** — i.e.,
  roughly 9 of 10 denials were **reversed on appeal** — and the system allegedly **counted
  on the fact that only a small fraction of patients appeal.** Denials that override
  physicians, banking on non-appeal.
- **Status (2026):** In March a federal court ordered **broad discovery** into UHC's
  AI-driven claims processes; the case is moving toward class certification and is one of
  the most-watched health-insurance cases of 2026.

## Why it is the perfect live example for Nick

- It is a **predictive model, not generative AI** — which cleanly separates Nick's argument
  from the LLM-hype conversation and lands it on decision-making systems generally.
- The alleged design — optimize denials, rely on people not appealing — is **accountability
  laundering**: no identifiable human answers for the specific decision; the model becomes
  the scapegoat and the patient bears the harm. It instantiates the responsibility gap
  (Matthias), the failure of meaningful human control (Santoni de Sio), the moral crumple
  zone (the pressured case managers), and the due-process failure (Citron & Pasquale) all at
  once.
- **Care in writing:** these are *allegations* in ongoing litigation. Attribute them as
  such ("the lawsuit alleges," "according to plaintiffs"). The ~90% figure and the
  "counted on non-appeal" characterization are plaintiffs' claims, not adjudicated fact.
