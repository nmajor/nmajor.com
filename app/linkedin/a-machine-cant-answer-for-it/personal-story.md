UnitedHealth automated the wrong thing. It wasn't even the fancy kind of AI. Now they're in court and named in a Senate report.

The company is pouring three billion dollars into new AI. But the tool in the lawsuit isn't part of that. It's an older prediction model called nH Predict, the boring sort nobody writes headlines about. It guessed how long a patient should need to recover, and the company leaned on it to decide who kept their coverage.

Over the years it did, denials for that care climbed from 8.7% to 22.7%, a Senate subcommittee found. One patient was 91, cut off while he was still recovering from a fall. His family paid about twelve thousand dollars a month until he died.

A machine can produce a decision. It can't be answerable for one. When it's wrong about a person's life, there's no one to hold to it, just a number everyone was measured against.

The staff who could have overruled it were reportedly pushed to stay within 1% of its guess. On paper there were humans in the loop. In practice there was just somewhere to put the blame.

Plenty of things are safe to automate. This wasn't one of them. And it isn't only a healthcare story. Insurance is going harder at AI than almost any other industry right now, and the firms out front need to take care where they point it.

So before you hand a machine a decision like that: when it gets one wrong, who answers for it?
