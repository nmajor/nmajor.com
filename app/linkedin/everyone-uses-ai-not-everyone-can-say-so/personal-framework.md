Banks mention AI least of any traditional industry in their filings. They also use it the most. That gap is a disclosure checklist.

It comes from a census by the Institute of Applied AI of 2,112 annual reports. What separates the loud sectors from the quiet ones is liability, not how much AI they run. Here is the checklist that gap teaches, worth running before you publish anything about your own AI.

1. Separate the two decisions. What you run is one choice. What you say about it in public is a different one, with different risks. In practice this is a short conversation between whoever runs the AI and whoever signs the disclosures, with legal and comms in the room.

2. For each public claim, ask what it commits you to if it turns out wrong. A filing, a press release, and a careers page are all on the record.

3. Map the claim to its liability. Does the AI touch a regulated or consumer-facing decision, like credit or a coverage denial? Are you describing more than the system does? This is not only a finance problem. A manufacturer advertising an "AI-driven quality system" it can't fully back has made a claim it may have to defend too.

4. Match your disclosure to that liability. Banks stay quiet near lending decisions on purpose. Insurers say more partly because a regulator obliges them to document it, not only because they are keen.

5. Never read saying-less as doing-less. In this data the quietest companies were often the heaviest users. Assume otherwise about a competitor and you are reading the wrong signal.

None of this is about going dark. It is about knowing which claims you would stand behind if one of them turned out wrong.
