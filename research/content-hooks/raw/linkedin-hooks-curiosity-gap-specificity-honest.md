## The Curiosity Gap (mechanism)
- Curiosity gap = "the uncomfortable tension that happens when your brain realizes there's something it wants to know but doesn't yet." Creates a loop the reader must close.
- The space between what readers know and what they want to know triggers them to seek the missing piece.

## Specificity beats drama (CORE anti-hype principle)
- Balance to aim for: "specific enough to matter, vague enough to intrigue."
- "Specificity beats drama" — "On a Tuesday in March" beats "Once upon a time" because concrete details earn trust faster than grand language.
- Acorns example (verbatim): **"41.29% of millennials spend more on coffee than their retirement accounts."** The EXACT figure — not "about 40%" — "makes it feel like a discovery, not a marketing line."

## Don't over-resolve the gap
- "The moment you resolve the gap in the hook itself, the curiosity is gone and so is the reader."
- An analysis of thousands of randomised headline experiments found MORE THAN HALF of headlines fail because they are TOO CONCRETE — they resolve the gap, leaving nothing to be curious about.
- So the craft is: be specific about the SETUP/stakes, but withhold the resolution/lesson.

## Honest vs clickbait (directly relevant to brand voice)
- "It's not about tricking people, it's about giving them a reason to pay attention."
- "Narrative hooks work by earning trust through honesty, not by sounding impressive."
- "Your hook should accurately reflect the essence of your content — a misleading or irrelevant hook can break that promise and damage your credibility."
- Sugarman echo: "you can have the best offer... and none of it matters if the first sentence doesn't earn the second one."

## Five hook types with real ad examples (Aviso Studios)
1. **Threat/Warning** — ADT Security: "26% of burglaries happen while someone is at home."
2. **Education (specific stat)** — Acorns coffee stat above.
3. **Confirm existing beliefs** — Organixx: "Many women know that hormonal imbalance can wreak havoc on their health." (mirrors belief rather than teaching)
4. **FOMO** — Blinkist: "Millions of tech-savvy intellectuals are using Blinkist." (NOTE: bandwagon framing — weaker/more hypey for skeptical B2B audience)
5. **Relevancy/timeliness** — Excedrin: "73% of Americans will have an election headache this year."

## ANNOTATION
- Credible-friendly takeaways: specificity-over-drama, exact numbers feel like discovery, withhold the resolution not the substance, "earn the second sentence."
- The "more than half of headlines fail because too concrete" finding is the nuance: a hook can be honest AND specific while still leaving an open loop (state the surprising fact/stakes, not the takeaway).
- AVOID for this brand: the FOMO/bandwagon type ("millions are using X"), and any "confirm belief" hook that flatters without substance.
