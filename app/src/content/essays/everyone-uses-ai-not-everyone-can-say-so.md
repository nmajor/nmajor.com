JPMorgan's data chief says some employees now [spend more on AI tokens than they earn in salary](https://www.americanbanker.com/news/how-banks-are-looking-to-tame-their-growing-ai-bills). The bank barely mentions AI in investor disclosures.

The Institute of Applied AI read all 2,112 traditional-company 2025 annual reports that mention AI and [graded what the language claims](https://www.appliedartificialintelligence.org/research/ai-in-10k-filings-2025/). Insurance mentioned AI in 82.9% of filings, above tech itself. Banks and lenders: 22.8%, the lowest of any traditional sector. That measures what a company will put on the record, not what it deploys.

JPMorgan has [450 generative-AI use cases in the works and put 200,000 employees on its internal assistant in eight months](https://tearsheet.co/artificial-intelligence/jpmorgan-chases-gen-ai-implementation-450-use-cases-and-lessons-learned/). PNC is [building its own GPU compute](https://www.americanbanker.com/news/how-banks-are-looking-to-tame-their-growing-ai-bills) rather than keep burning tokens. RBC's token use jumped 500% in a year. This is not an industry sitting AI out. It is one using it hard and saying as little as it can.

The silence is a choice, and a rational one. Almost everything a bank does with AI touches a regulated decision: who gets a loan, and at what rate. Put a model near that decision and you inherit fair-lending exposure, [which still runs through the Fair Housing Act and state laws](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/an-ai-fair-lending-policy-agenda-for-the-federal-financial-regulators/) even after this year's federal rollback. Add model-risk supervision, which the banks' own policy institute calls [among the most constraining rules on their adoption of technology](https://bpi.com/the-overlooked-risk-in-bank-ai-adoption-regulatory-inaction/). Add the SEC, which in 2024 [began going after companies that overstated their AI](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/9ab5047f/sec-heightens-enforcement-for-ai-related-disclosures). A bank is exposed both ways: say too much and you have named a fair-lending target; say too little and you have a disclosure problem. The safest sentence is the one you never write.

Insurance sits on the other side of that math. Insurance is prediction already, so pointing AI at underwriting or claims reads as doing the old job better, not doing something new and dangerous. Insurers are also increasingly [required to document it](https://content.naic.org/article/naic-members-approve-model-bulletin-use-ai-insurers). The NAIC's 2023 model bulletin tells them to keep a written AI program, and [Colorado now has that in statute](https://www.troutman.com/insights/locke-lord-quickstudy-artificial-intelligence-state-insurance-regulation-a-year-in-review/). When a regulator makes you describe your AI, disclosure stops being enthusiasm and becomes compliance. Some of insurance's lead is real appetite: [84% of health insurers say they already use AI](https://content.naic.org/article/naic-survey-reveals-majority-health-insurers-embrace-ai). Some of it is just a rule banks don't have.

So the gap is easy to misread. It looks like a map of who uses AI. It is really a map of who can afford to say so. What you run is an engineering and cost question. What you claim, in a filing or a press release, is a liability question, and the honest answer is sometimes less than what you have built.

If you run anything regulated, sit with that split before your next confident line about your AI. Not to go quiet for its own sake, but to know which claims you would stand behind if one turned out wrong. The gap between what a company runs and what it will admit to running will keep widening, and choosing where to sit in it is a strategy of its own. That is a lot of what I help regulated companies think through.

The reports were never a measure of who uses AI. Nearly everyone does, and soon every sector's mention rate will drift toward insurance's for reasons that have nothing to do with deployment. The filings measure something narrower and more useful: who has decided they can say so out loud. Everyone uses AI now. Not everyone can say so.
