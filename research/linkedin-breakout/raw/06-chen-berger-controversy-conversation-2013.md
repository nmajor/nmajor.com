Citation: Chen, Z., & Berger, J. (2013). "When, Why, and How Controversy Causes
Conversation." Journal of Consumer Research, 40(3), 580-593.

## Core findings (verbatim / near-verbatim)
- **There is an INVERTED-U (non-linear) relationship between controversy and
  conversation.** "Chen and Berger (2013) demonstrate a non-linear relationship
  between controversy and amount of subsequent discussion." Low controversy is
  boring; MODERATE controversy maximizes discussion; HIGH controversy suppresses it
  (people avoid talking about it because it becomes uncomfortable / socially risky).
- **The mechanism is two opposing forces:** "Controversial content produces both
  interest and discomfort simultaneously." Controversy raises interest (which drives
  conversation) but also raises discomfort (which inhibits it). The net effect
  depends on which dominates.
- **Moderate controversy boosts word of mouth "because it makes conversation more
  interesting"** (Berger, Annual Review of Psychology summary).
- **Identity disclosure flips the sign.** Discomfort — and therefore avoidance —
  rises sharply "especially when personal identity is disclosed." When people share
  UNDER THEIR OWN NAME (exactly LinkedIn's condition — no anonymity), they are far
  more reluctant to engage with high-controversy content because it reflects on them
  publicly. Anonymity reduces this inhibition; a real-name professional network
  amplifies it.
- Controversy is **audience-relative:** "what is controversial to some audiences may
  not be so to others." The same topic can be high-controversy in one community and
  neutral in another.

## Relevance to the breakout question (and the honesty limit)
This is the source that keeps the brand honest about "controversy as a reach lever."
On a real-name professional network like LinkedIn, the useful zone is MODERATE
controversy — a defensible, mildly against-the-grain position that a stranger feels
comfortable adding their own view to under their own name. HIGH controversy
(inflammatory hot takes, culture-war bait) actually SUPPRESSES the reshares/comments
you want, because identity disclosure makes people avoid it.

Post A's closing question — "who decided what it was allowed to see?" — is the
*productive* version: it invites a low-risk, identity-safe opinion (governance,
accountability) that a stranger WANTS to answer publicly because answering makes
them look thoughtful. Post C ("Ban AI at work and you do not remove the risk") is a
genuine contrarian position but framed as a flat assertion, not an open invitation —
it states the debate rather than handing the reader a comfortable seat in it. The
lesson: engineer a moderate, answerable point of tension the reader can safely
weigh in on; do not manufacture high-controversy outrage (which both suppresses
reach on a real-name network AND violates the anti-hype brand).
