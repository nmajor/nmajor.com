# Voice profile: Nicholas Major

This is Nick's own voice on nmajor.com — apply it to everything bylined **Nicholas
Major**, on top of the `writing-voice` core rules and the compact-for-busy-CEOs rule.
Built from 20 posts on his blog (nmajor.com); full analysis with evidence in
`research/writing-style/report.md`. The core rules always win where they conflict; this
tunes the personality inside them.

## The voice in one line

A senior engineer explaining something to a peer over coffee: first person, opinionated,
plain, the reason before the mechanics, and one dry aside. He tells you what he actually
did and what he thinks, and he names what he is unsure about.

## Keep these (they are what make it him)

- **First person, with an opinion.** "I went with Flux because it seemed a bit more
  lightweight." One clear position per piece. His credibility is a named person who did the
  thing and will tell you what he thinks.
- **Why before how.** A plain-language sentence of the idea before any detail. Model: "The
  idea behind GitOps is simple: your Git repository is the single source of truth."
- **Honest hedging and admitting limits.** "it seems to work out pretty well," "I know this
  is overkill." This is the anti-grandiosity brand in his own voice. Underclaim, name the
  uncertainty.
- **Plain language, contractions, sentences that open with but/so/and.** Already the house
  voice; he just does it naturally.
- **Punchy one-liners after a longer sentence.** "It works." "Enter GitOps." His four-word
  sentence after a long one is the rhythm we want.
- **One dry aside per piece.** A light, self-aware joke or admission makes him human (his
  "home-lab math: it feels free, it's not free, but it feels free"). One, not one per
  paragraph.
- **Sources credited by name, inline.** He always links what informed him. Keep it.

## Adapt these (his blog habits that fight our format)

- **Drop the tutorial scaffolding.** No "in this post I'm going to," no "In this post:"
  roadmap, no "Let's get started!", no "Wrap it up / Thanks for reading!" outro. Keep his
  straight-to-the-problem opener, lose the wind-up and the sign-off. End on the last fact.
- **Compress hard, with one exception.** His blog spends three paragraphs and a code block
  per idea because a tutorial earns it. Our posts state the idea, the reason, and the
  evidence in a few tight sentences. Short paragraphs. Well under 800 words. The exception:
  on the single load-bearing concept of a piece, he will spend words to specify it fully and
  plainly, even slightly redundantly. Cut everywhere else, not there.
- **No em dashes.** His recent writing leans on them. Replace every one with a period, comma,
  parentheses, or colon. Absolute.
- **Ration "basically."** He overuses it. One per piece at most, only where it genuinely
  compresses a concept.
- **No hype, even his casual kind.** His blog "amazing / magic / Holy Grail" enthusiasm reads
  as marketing here. Swap the excitement for a specific fact ("draws 11-12W idle," not
  "amazing").
- **Fix the mechanics.** His personal blog is loose with apostrophes and typos ("thats,"
  "isnt"). Keep the casual rhythm, clean the spelling and punctuation.
- **Sentence-case headings, concrete and short.** His "Why serverless," "What's next" already
  work; lowercase them. Drop Title Case and the bold-lead-in-on-every-bullet habit.

## Refinements from Nick's own edits (live piece, 2026-06-17)

When Nick edited a draft we wrote together, his changes all pointed the same way. Highest
signal, because it's him editing his own byline, not a years-old blog:

- **Plain over clever.** He strips writerly devices and compressed phrasing. "Read it as a
  point about order" became "It's about order." "produced token by token at a frontier
  model's price" became "And given the costs of some of the best models, it's expensive
  slop." If a line sounds crafted, flatten it to how he'd actually say it.
- **Name the concept, don't pronoun it.** "every handoff is a place where it thins out"
  became "an opportunity for the signal to thin or distort." He repeats the key term ("the
  signal") instead of "it" or a synonym, and adds precision ("thin or distort," "the person
  or system").
- **Spend words on the core idea.** He expanded the definition of "the signal" and added
  "they understand the work and they understand the context." On the one concept the piece
  rests on, fuller and plainer beats shorter. (See the compress exception above.)
- **Open with And / Or / So, and use casual intensifiers.** "And given the costs...", "Or
  making rookie mistakes...", "which things must absolutely be correct and perfect." Words
  like "rookie" and "absolutely" are his register.
- **Plain, relatable examples beat clever specific ones.** He swapped "why a date in the
  past should stop everything" for "where common mistakes happen in their work."
- **Break long sentences,** and **hedge for accuracy** ("the failures often cluster," not a
  sweeping absolute), which doubles as overclaim-guarding.

## Refinements from Nick's edits (build-vs-buy piece, 2026-06-19)

From a long line-by-line shaping session on "Building is now cheap. Owning isn't." All
high signal, him steering his own byline:

- **Bookend hard: the end rhymes with the beginning.** He wants a hook, then symmetry
  through the middle, then a close that calls back the opening. Land it with a title whose
  two halves the ending re-states. Here the title "Building is now cheap. Owning isn't."
  closed on "Building is cheap now. Owning is the part you have to mean." Build this in from
  the outline, don't bolt it on.
- **Punchy aphoristic pairs with an inversion.** He reaches for two short balanced
  sentences that flip a term: "Even software companies used to buy. Now even non-software
  companies can build." / "A wall does not work here. A paved road does." Give him the
  candidate, let him tune it.
- **More direct, less crafted.** When a transition sounded composed, he asked to "rephrase
  to be more direct" and to "give it some flare," meaning sharper and plainer at once, not
  ornate. Flatten writerly phrasing toward how he'd say it out loud.
- **Address the reader's action, don't abstract it.** "None of this is a reason to forbid
  it" became "This isn't a reason not to build." He turns an abstract framing ("forbid it")
  into the reader's concrete choice ("build"). Prefer the second-person, active version.
  (Watch the house ban: do not let this slide into "It's not X, it's Y".)
- **Audience first, and not coded for engineers.** He pulled the angle toward owners and
  operators of non-tech, brick-and-mortar businesses that pay a lot for SaaS, and cut a
  first-person "I build throwaway apps" opener as "too techy coded." His technical
  experience is better used as the trusted insider who delivers the warning than as a
  builder's anecdote up top. Hook on the reader's world (the software bill, a peer who
  saved money), bring his expertise in at the turn.
- **Always offer the hook and lede as options to choose**, never pre-commit a first line.
- **Source links woven richly throughout**, at each claim, are a standing expectation, not
  a nice-to-have. More verified inline links make the piece feel richer to him.

## Seven checkable rules

1. Open on the point or a concrete detail, never a roadmap.
2. Write in first person and commit to one position per piece.
3. State the why in plain words before any how.
4. Underclaim and name what's uncertain. Never round up.
5. Back every claim with a number, a named source, or a real example, inline. No adjective
   standing in for evidence.
6. Keep contractions, open sentences with but/so/and, vary length on purpose (long, then
   short). No em dashes.
7. End on the last fact, not a sign-off.
