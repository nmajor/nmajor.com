## IN MEDIAS RES ("into the middle of things")
============================================================

ORIGIN: Latin phrase coined by the Roman poet HORACE in Ars Poetica (~19 BC),
describing how Homer plunges the audience into the heart of the action rather than
starting "ab ovo" (from the egg / the beginning). One of the oldest documented hook
techniques in Western literature.

WHAT IT SAYS: Open in the MIDDLE of the action/conflict, before any setup or backstory.
Trust the audience to catch up. Provide just enough context for the scene to make
sense, but deliberately leave UNANSWERED QUESTIONS that the reader must continue to
resolve.

VERBATIM (from sources): "In medias res involves dropping into the middle of action
without explanation, trusting the reader to catch up." It "works by leaving the reader
with unanswered questions that they'll have to dig to find the answers for."

CONCRETE EXAMPLES:
- The Dark Knight opens mid-heist.
- Saving Private Ryan opens in the chaos of the Omaha Beach invasion.
- Homer's Iliad/Odyssey (the classical origin).
- TV/film convention: "a body discovered, a deal going sideways, a chase mid-sentence
  — hook first, explain later."

============================================================
## THE NARRATIVE HOOK & THE OPEN LOOP
============================================================

NARRATIVE HOOK (Wikipedia def, verbatim): "a literary technique in the opening of a
story that 'hooks' the reader's attention so that he or she will keep on reading."

THE OPEN LOOP (a.k.a. the Zeigarnik effect in practice):
- Psychological basis: the ZEIGARNIK EFFECT — Bluma Zeigarnik (1927) found people
  remember UNFINISHED/interrupted tasks better than completed ones; an open loop
  creates mental tension that demands closure.
- In copy/content: start a story or pose a question at the opening and DON'T resolve it
  immediately — the unresolved tension pulls the reader through the body until you
  close it. (Caples' "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano... But When I Started
  to Play!" is the canonical open-loop hook: the dash leaves the loop open.)
- Closely related to Loewenstein's information gap (see separate file): the open loop
  is the narrative implementation of a knowledge gap.

## THE SPECIFICITY / CONCRETENESS PRINCIPLE
============================================================

This is arguably the single most ANTI-HYPE-COMPATIBLE principle in all of copywriting:
specific, concrete details beat vague generalities AND superlatives — for both
credibility and memorability.

### Made to Stick — Chip Heath & Dan Heath (2007)
FRAMEWORK: SUCCESs = Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories.
"Concrete" and "Credible" are directly about specificity.

KEY CLAIMS (verbatim/near-verbatim):
- "Naturally sticky ideas are full of concrete images... our brains are wired to
  remember concrete data."
- On credibility through specifics: "concrete details don't just lend credibility to
  the authorities who provide them; they lend credibility to the idea itself."
- The Heaths warn about the "CURSE OF KNOWLEDGE": experts, once they know something,
  find it hard to imagine not knowing it, so they communicate in abstractions the
  audience can't grasp. The cure is concreteness.
- Their UNEXPECTEDNESS principle = open a "gap in [the audience's] knowledge — and then
  fill it" (their phrasing of the curiosity gap, citing Loewenstein). They advise:
  don't start with the answer; first make people aware they have a gap.

### Caples / Ogilvy on specificity (cross-ref):
- Caples: "Specifics sell better than generalities." Tested: "Lose 10 pounds in 30
  days" beats "Lose weight fast." Specific numbers are more BELIEVABLE.
- Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce headline: "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new
  Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock." — a single concrete fact outsells any
  superlative ("the quietest luxury car"). The detail IS the proof.

### Gary Halbert (Boron Letters) — on specificity & honesty
- Halbert preached using ultra-specific detail and "reason-why" copy; specifics make
  claims believable.
- "The Starving Crowd": "The only advantage I want is A STARVING CROWD!" — the
  market/desire matters more than the message. (Find a real, demonstrated existing
  demand; don't try to manufacture it — echoes Schwartz.) VERBATIM setup: it is far
  easier "to sell a below-average hamburger to a starving crowd than... the best
  hamburger in the world to a well-fed crowd."

WHY SPECIFICITY BEATS SUPERLATIVES (the principle): A superlative ("revolutionary,"
"world's best," "game-changing") asks the reader to TRUST a vague claim — which a
skeptic discounts to zero. A specific, concrete, checkable fact ("cut inference cost
37% by quantizing to int8") supplies its own evidence and survives scrutiny. Vague
words signal you have nothing concrete; specifics signal you actually did the thing.

============================================================
## ANTI-HYPE ASSESSMENT
============================================================
- TRANSFERS EXTREMELY WELL (the brand's core voice):
  * SPECIFICITY/CONCRETENESS — the strongest credible-friendly lever. Specific numbers
    and concrete details replace hype adjectives and earn a skeptic's trust.
  * The "curse of knowledge" cure (concreteness) — vital for a technical/expert audience.
  * Honest open loop / in medias res — opening with a real, specific scene or question
    you genuinely resolve. Story openings that are TRUE.
  * Halbert's "starving crowd" / Schwartz's channel-existing-desire — speak to a real,
    already-felt need; don't manufacture excitement.
- HANDLE WITH CARE:
  * Open loops / in medias res become BAIT if the payoff never comes or the mystery is
    manufactured — same failure mode as clickbait. The loop must close honestly.
  * Emotional agitation in storytelling must stay proportionate and true.
