Amazon's hiring AI taught itself to punish women's resumes.

Amazon trained it on ten years of past resumes, mostly from men, so the model learned to prefer men and started docking anyone whose resume mentioned a women's college or a women's team. They scrapped it before it ever chose a hire.

AI was fine writing the job post. The damage came from letting it rank the people. That's the line worth checking before you automate anything: is it doing a task, or making a decision?

Even recommending a decision or presenting a decision for a review can be troublesome.

Australia ran a version of this at national scale. Its Robodebt scheme used an algorithm to raise welfare debts, with a person meant to check each one before it went out. Over time the checking got stripped away until the notices went out with no real review at all. A Royal Commission later found the whole thing unlawful, and hundreds of thousands of people were chased for money they often never owed.

S&P Global says 42% of companies scrapped most of their AI initiatives last year, more than double the year before.

The ones that don't get tossed can be the bigger risk. The problem doesn't show up in the demo. It shows up later, as a lawsuit, a headline, or a Senate report. Toss it and you wasted the money. Ship it at the wrong thing and you bought a slow-moving liability.
