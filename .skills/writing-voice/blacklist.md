# Banned and flagged words and phrases

This is the reference list the `writing-voice` skill points to. Most of these are
**flags, not absolute bans** — a few ("help," "use") are unavoidable; the problem is
overuse and clustering. But the items marked **NEVER** are absolute, and as a rule, if
you find yourself reaching for anything below, there is a plainer word.

The deeper truth from the research: the word list is necessary but not sufficient.
Structure and rhythm give AI away more than any single word. Fix the skeleton first
(see `SKILL.md`), then clean the words.

## NEVER use these (absolute)

delve, leverage (verb), utilize, unlock, unleash, elevate, harness, empower,
revolutionize, supercharge, robust, seamless, seamlessly, game-changer, game-changing,
cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, best-in-class, paradigm shift, synergy, tapestry,
testament ("a testament to"), embark, "harness the power of," "unlock the potential,"
"take it to the next level," "move the needle," "at the forefront," "paving the way,"
"push the boundaries," "in today's fast-paced world," "in today's digital age," "in an
era where," "it's important to note," "it's worth noting," "needless to say,"
"in conclusion," "without further ado."

## Filler transitions (cut almost always)

Additionally, Moreover, Furthermore, Consequently, Subsequently, Hence, Thus,
Therefore, Nevertheless, Nonetheless, Notably, Importantly, Crucially, Essentially,
Ultimately, Indeed, Namely, Specifically (as a sentence-opener), Particularly,
Primarily, Conversely, Whilst, Albeit, Admittedly, Interestingly, Remarkably,
Undoubtedly, Obviously.

Use instead: but, so, and, also, still, though, or just start the sentence.

## Throat-clearing openers (delete, start on the point)

"When it comes to," "With that in mind," "That being said," "Having said that," "At the
end of the day," "As we navigate," "As technology continues to evolve," "At the heart
of," "In the realm of," "In this article we will," "Let's dive in," "Let's delve into,"
"First and foremost," "It goes without saying," "It is no secret that."

Suspense preambles that stall before the point (say the thing directly instead):
"Here's the part I keep coming back to," "Here's the part that," "The part I keep coming
back to is," "What I keep coming back to is," "Here's the thing," "Here's where it gets
interesting," "Here's what gets me," "But here's the kicker." If a line's only job is to
announce that a good sentence is coming, cut it and write the good sentence.

## Wrap-up closers (delete)

"In conclusion," "To wrap up," "To summarize," "In summary," "As we've seen," "It is
clear that," "All in all," "The bottom line is," "There you have it," "I hope this
helps," "Feel free to reach out," "Best of luck on your journey."

## Hype verbs

delve, unlock, unleash, elevate, embark, harness, leverage, utilize, empower,
revolutionize, transform (as hype), supercharge, catalyze, propel, spearhead, pioneer,
redefine, reshape, amplify, bolster, accelerate, streamline, foster, champion,
transcend, navigate (figurative), bridge ("bridge the gap").

## Hype phrases

"embark on a journey," "harness the power of," "unlock your full potential," "transform
the way you," "revolutionize the way," "change the game," "cutting-edge solution,"
"seamlessly integrate," "drive meaningful impact," "foster innovation," "take it to the
next level," "push the boundaries," "breaking new ground," "paving the way," "at the
forefront of," "leading the charge," "move the needle," "best of both worlds,"
"win-win," "the future of."

## Overused adjectives

robust, seamless, innovative, groundbreaking, transformative, revolutionary,
cutting-edge, bespoke, holistic, comprehensive, dynamic, vibrant, compelling,
captivating, crucial, pivotal, paramount, meticulous, profound, remarkable,
exceptional, unparalleled, unprecedented, powerful, impactful, sophisticated,
world-class, stellar, multifaceted.

## Pretentious nouns

tapestry, landscape (figurative), realm, journey (figurative), beacon, cornerstone,
pillar, pinnacle, nexus, mosaic, fabric, frontier, hallmark, ethos, zeitgeist,
trajectory, sphere, arena, terrain, labyrinth, testament, blueprint, ecosystem
(figurative), synergy, paradigm.

## Vague quantities

"a plethora of," "a myriad of," "a wide range of," "a wide variety of," countless,
numerous, myriad, "a vast array of," "a host of." Use a real number or a concrete list.

## Weasel attribution (name the source instead)

"experts argue," "observers have noted," "critics say," "some say," "studies show"
(without naming the study), "industry reports suggest," "it is widely believed,"
"many people," "research indicates" (un-cited).

## Empty intensifiers

absolutely, definitely, clearly, obviously, of course, undoubtedly, truly, really,
very, literally, surely, "without a doubt."

## Verb-phrase tells

"serves as," "stands as," "plays a crucial role in," "aims to," "designed to," "shed
light on," "dive deep into," "take a closer look," "cater to," "underscores,"
"highlights," "showcases," "boasts," "reflects a commitment to," "is a testament to."
Prefer plain "is/are" and a concrete verb.

## Constructions to ban (not words — patterns)

- "It's not X, it's Y" / "Not just X, but Y" / "Not X. Y."
- The rule of three (triplet lists and adjective triplets) used reflexively.
- "-ing" significance tails: "..., highlighting...", "..., underscoring...",
  "..., reflecting a broader...".
- "While X might seem..., Y" and "Sure, X. But Y."
- Em dashes as a writing device. (See `SKILL.md` — absolute ban.)
- Title Case Headings. Use sentence case.
