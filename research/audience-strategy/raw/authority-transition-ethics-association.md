## Source: testdouble.com — From Engineer to Consultant (inward to outward focus)
URL: https://testdouble.com/insights/from-engineer-to-consultant-the-powerful-shift-from-inward-to-outward-focus
- "engineering excellence is necessary, it's rarely sufficient for consulting success."
- Bridge via respect for existing systems: approach unfamiliar codebases with "a healthy respect"; legacy code "has proven itself in a legacy of successfully powering a business."
- Mindset shifts: self-focused -> client-focused ("the shift from an inward focus to an outward one"); judgment -> curiosity ("What circumstances led to this design?"); fighting constraints -> embracing them ("creative boundaries"), "What's the smallest change I can make to start adding value today?"
- Trust techniques: 1) Map before diving (high-level codebase map). 2) Find patterns not problems (suspend judgment; identify "cow paths"). 3) "Pop the why stack" — repeatedly ask "Why might they have done it this way?" uncovers hidden business requirements. 4) Translate between worlds ("the babel fish" between technical/non-technical). 5) Measure by pragmatic value — solving client's actual problem, not elegance.
- "The credential shift happens through demonstrating that technical expertise serves client outcomes, not ego."

## Source: WebSearch "ethics of omission vs lying" — KEY for honesty line
Links:
- https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/business-ethics-quarterly/article/lie-is-a-lie-the-ethics-of-lying-in-business-negotiations/F8049AA4BA0E9677401D6F274786B5EE
- http://imperfectcognitions.blogspot.com/2017/05/legitimate-lies-omission-commission-and.html
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12529216/
Key ethical distinctions:
- "Omission becomes lying when it satisfies the speech-act conditions of deception, and a special relationship (legal duty, fiduciary duty, explicit promise of full disclosure, or direct questioning) increases the obligation to disclose."
- Deception includes PALTERING (use of a factually truthful statement to create a false impression) and PASSIVE DECEPTION (misleading through omission) — "can... be as damaging as outright lies."
- Behavioral: people far more likely to lie by omission than commission; omission deemed "more acceptable, legitimate, and justifiable."
- Legal: failing to disclose material facts in contracts/securities/fiduciary relationships can be treated as fraud even with no explicit false statement.
- Principle re inexperience: "materiality and relational duties. If omission creates the same effect as an explicit falsehood, treat it as morally and often legally equivalent; when in doubt... disclose material facts or add qualifiers to avoid misleading impressions."
- "In marketing and professional services, failing to disclose material inexperience when a client relies on implied expertise would likely constitute unethical omission under these frameworks." <-- IMPORTANT LINE-DRAW

## Source: imperfectcognitions.blogspot.com — Legitimate Lies (Pittarello research)
URL: http://imperfectcognitions.blogspot.com/2017/05/legitimate-lies-omission-commission-and.html
- Andrea Pittarello research: "Participants were far more likely to lie by withholding the truth (omission) than by overtly reporting that the computer was wrong (commission)."
- "lies of omission were deemed as more acceptable, legitimate, and justifiable than those of commission."
- Implication: omission feels easier/more justifiable to the actor — a cognitive bias to GUARD AGAINST. Solution: increase transparency, limit situations where people passively profit from withholding.
NOTE/INTERPRETATION: This is descriptive psychology (people FIND omission more justifiable), NOT a normative endorsement that omission is ethical. Use to caution Nick that "it feels fine to omit" is exactly the bias that leads to crossing the line.

## Source: WebSearch "first case study / testimonial with no clients"
Links:
- https://janaomedia.com/get-clients-before-testimonials/
- https://www.consultingsuccess.com/consulting-testimonials
- https://learningresourcesonline.substack.com/p/how-to-build-proof-without-working
Key strategies (minimum viable proof):
- Leverage past experiences: write about solutions provided in previous roles; get testimonials from past colleagues/clients in different capacities.
- Use your own story / transformation as a case study — "demonstrate results without relying on client testimonials." (Build your own AI projects = case studies.)
- Run a BETA program: low-cost, limited-scope version of your service; deliver real value while testing system; collect feedback, clarity on pricing/positioning.
- Structure beta/pilot with clear goals e.g. "2 reference customers willing to provide a testimonial or case study."
- Caution: "avoid free work done out of guilt or fear." Do unpaid work to get process feedback, testimonials, confidence — not from fear.
