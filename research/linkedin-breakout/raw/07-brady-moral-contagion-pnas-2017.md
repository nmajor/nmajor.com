# Brady et al., "Emotion shapes the diffusion of moralized content" (PNAS, 2017) + replications

Sources:
- PNAS (primary): https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1618923114
- Pre-registered replication + meta-analysis (PNAS Nexus, 2023): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12578369
- Independent reanalysis (eScholarship): https://escholarship.org/content/qt990923xv/qt990923xv_noSplash_067ea6fbcc3e82b01c229264017b9356.pdf

Citation: Brady, W.J., Wills, J.A., Jost, J.T., Tucker, J.A., & Van Bavel, J.J.
(2017). "Emotion shapes the diffusion of moralized content in social networks."
PNAS, 114(28), 7313-7318.

## Core findings (verbatim / near-verbatim)
- Sample: 563,312 tweets on three polarizing moral/political topics (gun control,
  same-sex marriage, climate change).
- **"The presence of moral-emotional words in messages increased their diffusion by
  a factor of 20% for each additional word."** (mean IRR = 1.20 — a 20% increase in
  retweet rate PER moral-emotional word added), controlling for distinctly moral and
  distinctly emotional language separately.
- **Distinctly moral OR distinctly emotional words alone had much weaker effects; it
  is the COMBINATION (moral-emotional) that drives spread.** The interaction is the
  engine, not morality or emotion in isolation.
- **"Moral contagion was bounded by group membership."** Moral-emotional language
  increased diffusion much more strongly WITHIN ideological in-groups and less
  BETWEEN them. Shared-identity networks amplify; it does not cross group lines well.

## Robustness (this is why it's citable, not folklore)
- **Pre-registered direct replication + meta-analysis (2023): 5 labs, 27 studies,
  N = 4,821,006.** Pooled estimate IRR = 1.13 (95% CI 1.06-1.20) — i.e., ~13% more
  shares per additional moral-emotional word. The effect is smaller than the 2017
  original but positive, significant, and replicable at scale. (A couple of
  individual datasets showed null/negative effects — e.g., George Floyd — so it is
  domain-dependent, not a universal law.)

## Relevance to the breakout question
"Moral-emotional" framing is a robust, quantified diffusion driver. The CB Financial
story has a built-in moral-emotional core: a bank exposed ordinary customers'
Social Security numbers; an institution failed the people who trusted it; "who was
accountable?" That is a moral frame (fairness, responsibility, breach of trust)
fused with emotion (anxiety, indignation) — the exact combination that spreads.

TWO honest caveats for nmajor.com:
1. **Bounded by group membership** means moral framing spreads within a shared-
   values community, not universally — useful for a defined ICP, but not a magic
   broadcast button.
2. The anti-hype brand must use the mild, universally-shared moral frame
   (accountability, honesty, protecting customers) — NOT partisan/outrage moral
   framing, which is the outgroup-animosity engine (Rathje et al. 2021, cited in the
   Annual Review) that fuels the ugliest viral content and would poison the brand.
   Moral clarity, not moral outrage.
