# Robertson et al., "Negativity drives online news consumption" (Nature Human Behaviour, 2023)

Sources:
- Journal (primary): https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-023-01538-4
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36928780
- Nieman Lab summary: https://www.niemanlab.org/2023/03/negative-words-in-news-headlines-generate-more-clicks-but-sad-words-are-more-effective-than-angry-or-scary-ones
- Harvard Chan summary: https://hsph.harvard.edu/health-communication/news/negative-news-headline-more-clicks

Citation: Robertson, C.E., Pröllochs, N., Schwarzenegger, K., Pärnamets, P.,
Van Bavel, J.J., & Feuerriegel, S. (2023). "Negativity drives online news
consumption." Nature Human Behaviour, 7, 812-822.

## Method
Analyzed the Upworthy Research Archive — a dataset of ~105,000 real, randomized
A/B-tested headline variants and ~370 million impressions (Upworthy 2012-2015). This
is a rare CAUSAL design: same story, headlines varied at random, so differences in
click-through are attributable to the wording, not the story. Emotional language
scored via dictionary (NRC) methods.

## Core findings (verbatim / near-verbatim)
- **Negative words in headlines increase click-through; positive words decrease it.**
  "Consistent with the negativity bias hypothesis, the effect for negative words is
  positive (β = 0.015, ... P < 0.001)... a larger proportion of negative words in the
  headline increases the propensity of users to access a news story."
- **Effect size: each additional negative word in an average-length headline
  (~15 words) raised click-through rate by ~2.3%.** A one-SD increase in the
  proportion of negative words raised click odds ~1.5%.
- **Positive words hurt:** the coefficient for positive words is NEGATIVE
  (β = -0.008, P < 0.001) — more positive words lowered click-through.
- **Which negative emotion matters most:** breaking negativity into discrete
  emotions, **SADNESS-related words most reliably increased clicks**; anger- and
  fear/disgust-related words were associated with FEWER clicks in this dataset.
  (Nieman Lab: "sad words are more effective than angry or scary ones.")

## IMPORTANT reconciliation with Berger & Milkman (file 01)
These two landmark studies measure DIFFERENT behaviors and partly disagree — do not
conflate them:
- **Robertson 2023 measures CONSUMPTION (clicking to read).** Here, negativity
  (esp. sadness) drives the click. Low-arousal sadness works to get a click.
- **Berger & Milkman 2012 measures SHARING (most-emailed).** There, high-AROUSAL
  emotion (awe, anger, anxiety) drives the share, and low-arousal SADNESS SUPPRESSES
  it.
- So the honest synthesis: **fear/anxiety/negative framing helps you get the click
  and the attention (consumption); high-arousal activation — including anxiety and
  anger but NOT sadness — is what converts attention into the RESHARE that produces
  breakout.** A cautionary/risk story pairs a negative, attention-grabbing frame
  (consumption lever) with anxiety/surprise arousal (sharing lever) — which is why
  the genre travels. A doom-and-gloom SAD post might get clicks but won't get
  reshared.

## The guardrail this source forces
The negativity effect is real but the paper's own authors and the Harvard/Nieman
write-ups flag the societal cost: optimizing for negativity "desensitizes people to
truly bad news, spreads doomsday thinking, and erodes trust." For an ANTI-HYPE brand
whose entire wedge is honesty and calm, leaning on fear is the single most dangerous
temptation the breakout data creates. Fear-as-frame is available but must be
rationed and always paid off with a constructive, non-catastrophizing take — or the
brand becomes exactly the hype-merchant (in negative polarity) it positions against.
