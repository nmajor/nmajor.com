# 04 — Teardowns & Case Studies: Excellent B2B Founder/Operator LinkedIn Content

Raw research dump. Topic: teardowns and case studies of high-performing, credibility-led,
"build/learn in public," educational, anti-hype B2B LinkedIn content we can learn STRUCTURE
from. Scope per brief: text posts + document/PDF carousels, from credible operators,
consultants, fractional execs, and applied-AI practitioners.

Collected: 2026-06-29. Sources are web search + WebFetch of articles. **CAVEAT ON ENGAGEMENT
NUMBERS:** most "X likes / Y% engagement" figures below come from third-party teardown blogs
(swipe-file / agency content). They are reproduced as the source stated them but are
**UNVERIFIED** against LinkedIn itself. They are directional folklore, not audited fact. The
two exceptions noted inline are scraped from the live LinkedIn post pages. Treat all
single-post virality claims with suspicion — survivorship bias is heavy in this genre.

## A. THE STRONGEST EVIDENCE FOR OUR ANTI-HYPE WEDGE (Edelman–LinkedIn 2025)

This is the load-bearing data behind the whole "credibility, not hype" positioning. Sourced,
not folklore — it's the annual Edelman/LinkedIn B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report (≈2,000
global professionals surveyed, incl. "hidden" decision-makers).

- 71% of hidden buyers say thought leadership is **more effective than traditional
  marketing/sales materials** at demonstrating a vendor's potential value.
- 64% trust thought leadership **more than product sheets or brochures** when assessing
  capability.
- 95% of hidden buyers say strong thought leadership makes them **more receptive to sales
  outreach**.
- 79% of hidden decision-makers are **more likely to champion a vendor** with consistent,
  high-quality thought leadership during the RFP.
- 81% say high-quality thought leadership helped them understand a **previously unrecognized
  challenge or opportunity** (the "name the problem they didn't know they had" move).
- 63% of hidden buyers spend **>1 hour/week** consuming thought leadership.
- 40%+ of B2B deals stall due to internal misalignment — TL's job is to arm internal champions.
- The report's recurring warning: **low-quality thought leadership actively erodes trust and
  loses business.** Volume is a liability if the substance is thin.

## B. DECONSTRUCTED POST EXAMPLES (with structure, hook → body → close)

### B1. The "I reviewed N things, here are the ones that stood out" curator post
**Author:** Lewis Walker (LinkedIn, applied-AI-for-execs newsletter operator)
**Post:** "I reviewed 3,427 AI case studies. These 40 stood out:"
**Engagement (SCRAPED FROM LIVE POST — more credible than blog figures):** ~718 reactions,
~559 comments. Edited ~10 months before capture.

Structure:
- **Hook:** A big specific number + a ruthless filter. "I reviewed 3,427 AI case studies.
  These 40 stood out:" The number signals labor (authority by volume of work done) and the
  filter ("these 40 stood out") promises curation — he did the sifting so you don't have to.
- **Body:** A list of 40 named enterprise case studies (McKinsey, Meta, Amazon, Uber, Pfizer
  drug-development acceleration, Shopify listing optimization, Deutsche Telekom training
  personalization, etc.). Each is a NAMED COMPANY + NAMED OUTCOME. No adjectives doing the
  work — the specificity is the credibility.
- **Close:** Invites contribution ("Any case studies you'd add?") + soft newsletter CTA.
- **Why it works / what to copy:** The post is anti-hype by *construction* — it's a filtered
  list of real implementations with measurable outcomes, explicitly positioned against the
  "oversaturated AI landscape." Comments reward the meta-point that the best cases were about
  decision-pipeline redesign, not the technology. This is almost exactly our beat. The
  reproducible move: **do real volume of homework, filter hard, name names and numbers, invite
  additions.** This is the single most on-brand example found.
- Source: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/lewiswalkerai_i-reviewed-3427-ai-case-studies-these-40-activity-7358829330661031936-AW2U

### B2. Ten measured/deconstructed posts (magicpost.in teardown)
Source: https://magicpost.in/blog/linkedin-post-examples — figures are the blog's, UNVERIFIED.
Useful less for the celebrity authors than for the *structural pattern per post type*:

1. **The Win — Alex Hormozi.** Hook: "Thanks to you all. We broke a Guinness World Record."
   Structure: credits audience → demotes the achievement to a milestone → reveals a bigger
   mission. Lesson: wins perform when they point *outward*, reframed as progress not bragging.
   (Claimed 8,110 likes.)
2. **The Story — Tobi Oluwole.** Hook: "I came to Canada against my will in 2010." Opens on
   tension → turning point → unresolved cliffhanger before the "see more" fold. Lesson: stories
   live or die on first-line tension; cliffhangers earn the expand.
3. **The Lesson — Tobi Oluwole.** Hook: "One of my reps got an insane job offer." Confesses a
   selfish instinct → shows the generous action → the gap between them *is* the insight. Lesson:
   confessing the ugly thought first makes the wisdom feel earned, not preached. (Directly
   relevant to honest/"show your work" authority.)
4. **The Launch — Simon Sinek.** Hook: "Getting the title is the easy part." Describes a pain
   the reader already feels → delays the product name to the final line. Lesson: solve the
   problem first, position product as relief, never lead with the product.
5. **The Contrarian/Satire — Ken Cheng.** Escalates absurdity line by line → punchline inverts
   the premise. (A satire format; less relevant to us — we don't want to be the joke account.)
6. **The Explainer — Ruben Hassid.** Hook: "BREAKING: Disney is suing Midjourney for $29M."
   Borrows news urgency → numbered breakdown → zooms to the stakes. Lesson: doing the math
   transparently removes reader effort and makes complex news shareable.
7. **The Tips List — Hormozi.** Hook: "Instead of trying to get rich. Do this." Inverts the
   cliché → numbered self-contained rules. Lesson: inverted hooks refresh tired topics; numbered
   items invite readers to extend the list in comments.
8. **The Positive Message — James Caan.** Abstract sentiment → anchored to a concrete famous
   example (Ford & Edison). Lesson: universal messages need a *particular image*; specifics beat
   generic sentiment.
9. **The Quick Tip — Simon Sinek.** Hook: "Don't be fooled by a 'passionate' candidate." One
   counterintuitive claim → one reason → reframe → stop. Lesson: one sharp idea travels; over-
   stuffing kills it.
10. **The Value-Sale — Justin Welsh.** Hook: "Only foolish people dismiss hard work as 'luck.'"
    Names a reader belief → dismantles it with specifics → no pitch. Lesson: value-first builds
    authority without asking; credibility compounds.

**Common thread the teardown itself names (high signal):**
(1) first lines create tension/claim, never warm up; (2) concrete details — numbers, names,
moments — over generality; (3) ONE central idea per post; (4) endings invite a response rather
than conclude.

## C. OPERATOR / CONSULTANT AUTHORITY SYSTEMS (the 8-figure GTM teardown)

Source: https://startupgtm.substack.com/p/5-linkedin-systems-generating-8-figure
This is the most useful teardown for *how credible operators build authority without
overclaiming.* Five systems; the first two are most on-brand for an anti-hype institute.

### C1. Chris Walker / Refine Labs (and successor brands) — the data-backed contrarian
- **Move:** identify an industry pain, challenge conventional wisdom WITH DATA, position the
  contrarian POV as the path forward.
- **Signature POV:** "Attribution is fundamentally broken; brand drives demand." Repeated,
  consistent, defensible.
- **Authority mechanism:** weekly analysis of 50+ marketing-leader posts; surfaces the gap
  between what's said and what's true; backs it with proof points. Authority comes from
  *pattern recognition across a corpus*, not from claims about himself.
- **Copy for us:** pick 1–2 spiky-but-defensible POVs about applied AI ("most AI pilots fail for
  org reasons, not model reasons") and return to them weekly with fresh evidence. Consistency of
  POV = authority.

### C2. Katelyn Bourgoin / Why We Buy — self-referential proof
- **Move:** teach a principle (buyer psychology), then prove it by applying it to her own content
  and SHOWING THE RESULTING METRICS. "Compounding credibility."
- **Templates she reuses:**
  - "Mistake Reveal": *"I analyzed 847 customer interviews and found this shocking pattern…"*
    (big specific N + a finding).
  - "Before/After Transformation": principle applied → conversion metric shown.
  - "Counterintuitive Truth": challenge a marketing belief with research.
- **Authority mechanism:** she doesn't claim expertise, she *demonstrates* it on herself and
  reports the number. Show-don't-tell in its purest form.
- **Copy for us:** when we test an AI workflow internally, publish the number. "Here's what it
  did to our own X." The proof is that we ran it on ourselves.

### C3. Amanda Natividad / SparkToro — engagement ladder + "engagement assets"
- **Move:** value-first commenting ladder (observation → value-add → data-share → collaboration
  → DM). Drops specific PDFs/datasets/frameworks in comments as "engagement assets" that become
  natural conversation bridges. Resource-provider posture, never seller.
- **Copy for us:** our reports/frameworks ARE the engagement assets. Reference and offer them as
  resources, not pitches.

### C4. Gong — content multiplication ("Content DNA extraction")
- One core insight → 10 assets (doc, carousel, video, newsletter, webinar, sales opener, case
  study). Depth signalled by format diversity. (Relevant to our repurposing motion, not authority
  per se.)

### C5. Drift — native conversion architecture
- Comment-triggered lead magnets, single-field forms, *peer-learning events not vendor pitches*,
  "here's how Company X solved this" instead of direct pitches. Event-based credibility (claimed
  60% attendance, 20%+ to sales conversations — UNVERIFIED).
- **Copy for us:** the "here's how a named company solved this" framing > self-promotion.

**Cross-cutting pattern the teardown names:** Point of View → Content Operations → Engagement
Systems → Demand Architecture. Authority starts with a defensible POV, not tactics.

## D. THE "HONEST MISTAKE / WHAT DIDN'T WORK" POST (show-don't-tell authority)

### D1. Framework for a trust-building mistake post (The Trusted Voice)
Source: https://blog.thetrustedvoice.co/blog/content/linkedin-mistake-story-content/
Four-part structure:
1. **The Mistake** — state what you got wrong, *specifically*.
2. **The Lesson** — the insight extracted.
3. **How it changed you** — growth/resolution.
4. **The takeaway for the reader** — something they can apply.
- Rationale: "LinkedIn is drowning in success porn"; vulnerability + specificity create trust.
- Example template line: *"I used to think showing personality in my work would make me look
  less professional. Now? It's the reason clients say they trust me."*
- **Guardrails (important for our voice):** don't share unprocessed trauma, blame narratives,
  or anything that damages core professional credibility. The "dinner-party test": if the story
  would cause an awkward silence, it's too raw. Generic mistakes read as inauthentic — the
  detail is the whole point.

### D2. Real example — Braydon Moreno (Robo 3D), failure post
Reproduced specifics (from search excerpt): *"we launched the Robo R2 and Robo C2 3D printers to
the market before they were 100% ready. The product didn't deliver on the entire expectations
that we had set. We had countless shipping issues. The software was buggy. The machine had some
fail points."*
- **Why it works:** named product SKUs, named failure modes (shipping, buggy software, fail
  points). No hedging, no spin. The specificity IS the credibility. This is the texture we want
  when we write "here's what didn't work."
- Source: https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/least-likely-thing-post-linkedin-my-story-failure-braydon-moreno

**Copy for us:** the honest-failure post is structurally simple (mistake → lesson → change →
reader takeaway). The differentiator is *concrete nouns and numbers*, and a takeaway that makes
the confession useful rather than performative.

## E. DOCUMENT / PDF CAROUSEL ANATOMY (the formats execs SAVE)

Carousels/document posts are repeatedly cited as the highest-saved, highest-dwell native format.
**FLAG:** the often-quoted "document posts hit 6.60% engagement, the highest of any format" and
"carousels get 3.7x reach" figures are agency-blog numbers (Oktopost / meet-lea / postiv /
dataslayer), **UNVERIFIED and likely cherry-picked.** Direction is plausible (saves + dwell are
strong signals); exact multipliers are not trustworthy. Personal-profile carousels reportedly
out-engage company-page carousels (one source: "63% higher") — again directional only.

Eight carousel archetypes (source: https://thisisdcp.com/8-linkedin-carousel-post-examples-that-work-in-2025/),
with the slide skeleton. The ones execs SAVE are the framework / how-to / data ones:

1. **Step-by-step / How-To** — Slide 1: attention title; Slides 2–N: ONE step per slide; final:
   CTA question. Works via a "curiosity gap" pulling the swipe. Best for demonstrating expertise.
2. **Myth-Busting** — Slide 1: the myth framed provocatively; body: truth + research per myth;
   final: engagement Q. Builds credibility through education. (Pairs perfectly with anti-hype.)
3. **Before & After (Transformation)** — Slide 1: starting challenge WITH DATA; body: process +
   key strategies; final: results + CTA. Best for consultants showing ROI.
4. **Unconventional Perspective** — Slide 1: bold contrarian statement; body: argument built
   step-by-step WITH DATA; final: "Am I off-base?" debate invite.
5. **List / Framework** — Slide 1: numbered/themed title; body: one point per slide, consistent
   visuals; final: CTA for input. **This is the canonical "saved" format** — neatly organized,
   high retention.
6. **Experience / Lessons Learned** — Slide 1: vulnerable hook ("I completely botched my last
   project"); body: specific lesson per slide; final: community Q. Humanizes, builds connection.
7. **Trends & Predictions** — Slide 1: trend headline; body: one prediction + stat per slide;
   final: opinion Q. Positions as forecaster.
8. **Quick Tips / Instant Wins** — Slide 1: promise title; body: one executable tip per slide,
   minimal text; final: Q. Low cognitive load → saves/shares.

Universal carousel success factors (the source's own list): strong opening hook establishing
relevance; visual consistency; minimal text / maximum clarity per slide; a CTA that invites
discussion; data or specific examples backing every claim.

Carousel construction rule of thumb across sources: **one idea per slide, 5–10 slides, the cover
slide is the whole ballgame** (it's the only thing most people see in-feed), and the value must
be *referenceable later* — that's what earns the save. The "save" is the conversion event for
educational content because saves are among LinkedIn's strongest 2026 ranking signals.

Sources: https://www.oktopost.com/blog/linkedin-carousel-pdf-best-practices/ ,
https://meet-lea.com/en/blog/linkedin-carousel-examples ,
https://postiv.ai/blog/linkedin-carousel-examples-2

## F. WHAT'S WORKING vs FALLING FLAT FOR AI CONTENT (2025–2026)

Sources:
- https://www.zoomsphere.com/blog/linkedin-algorithm-2026-why-generic-ai-content-kills-your-organic-reach
- https://www.linkboost.co/blog/linkedin-algorithm-changes-2026/ (note: returned 403 on fetch;
  details below are from the search summary, not the full article — treat as secondary)
- https://contentmarketinginstitute.com/social-media-content/linkedin-algorithm-marketing-strategy
- https://www.dataslayer.ai/blog/linkedin-algorithm-february-2026-whats-working-now

**FALLS FLAT:**
- **Generic AI-written content with no specific professional insight at its core.** The stated
  failure mechanism is not "LinkedIn detects AI" (it reportedly can't reliably) — it's that
  **nobody finishes reading**, so near-zero dwell time, zero saves, no discussion → no
  distribution. Dwell time / "Depth Score" is the gatekeeper.
- **Engagement bait.** A 2026 "Authenticity Update" reportedly penalizes "Comment YES if you
  agree!"-style solicitations via NLP classifiers that cap distribution. (FLAG: specific
  mechanism is agency-reported, not confirmed by LinkedIn. Direction — bait is dying — is widely
  echoed.)
- **Pods / artificial engagement** reportedly losing reach; distribution reallocated to "Depth
  Score" content.
- Overly promotional / company-page content: one figure cited — organic company content shows in
  ~2% of feeds vs ~31% for top personal creators (UNVERIFIED but echoed widely; supports the
  founder-led, personal-profile strategy).

**BREAKS THROUGH (non-hype positioning):**
- Substance over spectacle. "Serious professionals … are looking for frameworks, not hot takes.
  Principles, not prompts. Wisdom, not hype." (paraphrase the genre keeps repeating — this is
  almost a literal statement of our wedge.)
- Research-backed challenges to conventional wisdom; memorable mental models and frameworks that
  support a decision.
- Threads where 2–3 named professionals add distinct perspectives from their own work beat ten
  "great post!" replies (substantive comments are the signal now).
- Specificity, proprietary data, and named outcomes (see Lewis Walker, Bourgoin).
- Video reportedly gets ~3x impressions of text-only (directional; a format note, not a
  positioning note).

**Net for us:** the platform's 2025–26 drift toward dwell-time/depth and away from bait is
*structurally favorable to an anti-hype institute.* Our liability is thin content, not honesty.
The winning unit is: a defensible POV + specific evidence + a framework worth saving, on a
personal founder profile.

## G. SYNTHESIZED STRUCTURAL LESSONS (what to actually copy)

1. **Hook = tension or a specific claim, never a warm-up.** Land it before the ~210-char "see
   more" fold. Best on-brand patterns for us: (a) the "I reviewed/analyzed N, here's what held
   up" curator hook; (b) the contrarian "everyone says X; the data says Y"; (c) the honest "here's
   what didn't work, and the number."
2. **Specificity is the credibility.** Named companies, named SKUs, named failure modes, real
   numbers. Adjectives don't build authority; nouns and figures do. This is the through-line of
   every genuinely credible example (Walker, Bourgoin, Moreno).
3. **One idea per post / one idea per slide.** Overstuffing kills both text posts and carousels.
4. **Show-don't-tell via self-application.** Run the AI workflow on ourselves, publish the result
   and the number (Bourgoin's compounding-credibility move). Authority by demonstration > claims.
5. **Honest-failure structure:** mistake (specific) → lesson → what changed → reader takeaway.
   Keep it useful, not confessional; pass the dinner-party test.
6. **Confess the ugly thought before the lesson** (Oluwole) — makes wisdom feel earned, defuses
   the "preachy expert" read that skeptical CTOs hate.
7. **Carousels are for the saveable framework.** Cover slide does ~all the work; 5–10 slides, one
   point each, data on every claim, the artifact is referenceable later. Saves are the conversion.
8. **A consistent, defensible POV is the real authority engine** (Walker). Pick 1–2 spiky-but-
   true positions on applied AI and return to them with fresh evidence weekly.
9. **End by inviting a substantive response, not "comment YES."** Engagement bait is now penalized;
   ask a question only a practitioner could answer.
10. **The "here's how [named company] solved this" frame beats self-promotion** (Drift). Our
    case-study library is the asset; teach from it.
