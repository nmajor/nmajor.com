## What they did
Baker Hughes deployed C3 AI Sourcing Optimization across its procurement organization to fight
fragmented data: critical sourcing information was scattered across disconnected systems and buyers
reconciled it by hand in spreadsheets. The system pulls 30M+ records from five enterprise sources
and surfaces sourcing opportunities to buyers with total-landed-cost breakdowns, similar-part
rationale, and index-based pricing signals, plus live tariff feeds. Buyers accept or reject each
recommendation (human-in-the-loop); the model learns from that feedback. Rolled out from a 3-site
pilot (2019) to 10 sites and then enterprise-wide.

## The numbers
- 900,000 SKUs supported; 800+ sites at full scale (vendor-reported).
- "Millions" in economic benefit in CY2025, no precise figure given (vendor-reported).
- 30M+ records integrated from 5 data sources; 200+ active users.
- 5-7% acceptance rate on flagged cost-saving opportunities (vendor-reported).

## What broke / the caveats
This is a C3 AI customer story, i.e. marketing. The headline benefit is the vague "millions," with
no dollar figure, baseline, or ROI math, so the financial claim is soft. The genuinely useful and
unusually honest number is the 5-7% acceptance rate on flagged opportunities: most AI sourcing
suggestions are declined by buyers, which is a realistic picture of human-in-the-loop adoption and
worth keeping. This was a multi-year program (2019 onward), not a quick win, and "AI" here is
recommendation/optimization on top of a large data-integration effort.

## Why it matters for our beat
A global industrial company putting AI into procurement, one of the least glamorous and most
spreadsheet-bound back-office functions, with a real human-in-the-loop design and a candid
acceptance rate. The 5-7% figure is a rare honest signal in a vendor case study.
