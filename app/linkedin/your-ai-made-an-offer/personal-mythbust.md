Air Canada told a tribunal it wasn't responsible for what its own chatbot said. It lost.

Jake Moffatt's grandmother died. He went to Air Canada's website, and its chatbot told him he could book now and apply for the bereavement discount afterwards.

That was wrong. It contradicted the airline's own policy page, which the chatbot had linked to.

He bought $1,630.36 in tickets, applied for the refund, and was refused. So he took the airline to a small-claims tribunal in British Columbia, and won $812.02.

Small money, and worth being precise about what it is. A small-claims tribunal decision binds nobody else. It isn't precedent. What makes it useful is the reasoning.

Air Canada argued it could not be held liable for information provided by its own chatbot. It did not explain why. Tribunal member Christopher Rivers called that submission remarkable, and wrote that the chatbot "is still just a part of Air Canada's website."

You may have seen this case go past with a better line attached, that Air Canada argued the chatbot was "a separate legal entity that is responsible for its own actions."

The airline never said that. Those are Rivers' words, describing an argument the airline wouldn't spell out.

One more thing that gets stretched. Rivers decided it as negligent misrepresentation, not contract. It doesn't turn your bot's offers into binding contracts. It makes you answerable for what your bot tells people, which is the one that reaches you first.

That's roughly how most of what we repeat about AI and liability got its confidence.

Worth reading the primary source on the ones you're relying on.
