# Writer voice analysis: Nick Major

> Subagent analysis of 20 posts from nmajor.com (raw bodies in `raw/`). The 2018 tutorials
> are his natural pre-AI voice; the 2025 home-lab Kubernetes series shows where it matured.
> The usable, distilled profile lives in `.skills/writing-voice/voice-nick.md`. This is the
> evidence behind it. Quotes are verbatim.

## Register and tone
Casual, peer-to-peer, a developer narrating over your shoulder. Writes to "you" as a fellow
builder, shares what worked for him rather than issuing decrees. "Here is the approach I've
used recently and it seems to work out pretty well." "Linters can be annoying at first, but I
feel like they really help me write better code." Light exclamation: "It works!", "Bingo!",
"Pretty easy."

## First-person habits
"I" is everywhere, and it is opinion-bearing. "I feel like it is more inline with the core
component philosophy of react." "I prefer Styled Components because..." "I don't think I've
ever had so much trouble with such a popular module." Comfortable narrating his own process
and missteps: "in an effort to find a quick fix I just committed my built files which sucks."

## Sentence rhythm and length
Mostly short-to-medium declaratives. Varies naturally: a run of explanation, then a punchy
one-liner. "It works!" / "Enter GitOps." / "It feels free. It's not free. But it _feels_
free." Longer sentences are loose and additive, strung with "and"/"so." Paragraphs are short,
often one to three sentences.

## How he opens
1. Straight to the problem: "Express.js doesn't come with es6 out of the box. But its actually
   not that hard to add." "The goal of this project is to build a Kubernetes cluster in my
   home lab out of HP EliteDesk mini machines."
2. A playful hook when excited: "What's the opposite of the boy who cried wolf? The boy who
   cried that something amazing was coming? The boy who cried free pizza?"
Often followed by an "In this post:" roadmap and "Let's get started!" (both to be cut for us).

## How he explains
Why-first, then how, heavy use of "basically" to compress: "This subscription is basically a
billing program." "The idea behind GitOps is simple: your Git repository is the single source
of truth. Want to install something? Commit a file." Plain-language definitions before code;
everyday analogies.

## Humor and personality
Dry, self-aware, never overdone. The free-pizza opener. Self-deprecation: "I just committed my
built files which sucks." The "home-lab math" riff: "It feels free. It's not free. But it
_feels_ free." Throwaway enthusiasm: "Thats pretty neat." "Also home-labbing is just fun."

## Connective tissue / plain language
Contractions throughout (loose with apostrophes: "thats," "isnt"). Opens sentences with "But,"
"So," "And," "Then." Filler that signals informality: "basically," "kind of," "a bit," "and
stuff." Casual intensifiers: "really," "pretty," "totally."

## Hedging and opinion
Takes positions, frames them as his own experience. "it seems to work out pretty well." "for
now I'm going with this simple subscribe approach." Strong when he means it: "We should NOT
rely on developers writing queries and logic to keep this data isolation."

## How he closes
Short wrap, honest assessment, invitation to correct him: "As always, please comment below if
you see any room for improvement." "I hope this helps someone." "So give Styled Components a
try!" (For us: cut the sign-off, end on the last fact.)

## Recurring tics
"basically," "I feel like," "pretty [easy/well/neat]," "as you can see," "Lets [verb]," "Thats
it!", "and stuff," "I whipped up," "works like a charm," roadmap lines ("In this post:", "The
plan:"). Approval words: nice, clean, neat, cool, fun.

## Formatting
Short, action/topic-named headings ("Break it down," "Why serverless," "What's next"). Lists
for definitions/plans/pros-cons; 2025 bolds the lead word of each bullet (a tell to drop).
Code-first cadence: setup sentence, code block, "As you can see..." Inline links crediting
sources by name. Bold for warnings.

## Evolution 2018 to 2025
Core voice stable. By 2025: more narrative/personal framing up front, cleaner mechanics,
numbered step lists, explicit series cross-links, and he picked up the em-dash habit ("it's
empty — and I don't want to..."). The em dash is the single biggest thing to strip when
adapting his voice, since the house bans it.

## Strongest natural-voice exemplar
`raw/post-2018-serverless-back-end-for-react-your-introduction-to.txt` (free-pizza opener,
why-first explanation, comment-driven close).
