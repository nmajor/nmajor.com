If your team is building its own AI tools, three rules keep it from turning into a data leak.

Cheap AI tools mean more companies are building their own internal software, often without IT in the loop. In Retool's 2026 build-versus-buy report, 60% of people who had built an internal tool said they did it outside any IT oversight. That number surprised me.

If it's happening at your company, three rules keep a useful tool from becoming a liability.

1. Give it one sanctioned place to build, in the open where you can see it. The risky version is the tool someone spins up on a personal account, out of view.

2. Base the review on the data, not the size. A meeting-notes summarizer is low risk. A customer tracker holding names, addresses, and card numbers is not, however fast it was built. That one gets a real security review.

3. Give anything that lasts a named owner. Buy software and the vendor patches the security holes for you. Build your own and that job is yours, or no one's. The unowned tool is the one that fails quietly, still holding real data.

If you sign the software checks: could you name every tool your team has built this year, and what data each one holds?
