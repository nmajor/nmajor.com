# Berger & Milkman, "What Makes Online Content Viral?" (JMR, 2012)

Primary source (MSI working paper version of the paper, full findings):
https://thearf-org-unified-admin.s3.amazonaws.com/MSI/2020/06/MSI_Report_10-114.pdf

Journal citation: Berger, J., & Milkman, K. L. (2012). "What Makes Online Content
Viral?" Journal of Marketing Research, 49(2), 192-205. (~6,400+ Google Scholar
citations as of 2025 — one of the most-cited marketing papers ever; won Sage's
10-Year Impact Award.)

Related priming source: https://datapsych.net/blog-virality (secondary summary)
Google Scholar (citation counts): https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=jGzE0DAAAAAJ

## Method
Analyzed all New York Times articles published online over a ~three-month period
(~7,000 articles). Outcome: whether each article made the NYT "most emailed" list.
20% of articles in the final dataset earned a spot on the most-emailed list. Human
coders (blind to hypotheses) rated each article for the emotions it evoked
(anxiety, anger, awe, disgust, sadness, surprise) and for practical utility /
interest. Controlled for placement, timing, section, author, etc.

## Core findings (verbatim / near-verbatim from the paper)
- "Affect-laden content — regardless of whether it is positive or negative — is
  more likely to make the most emailed list." Emotionality itself drives sharing.
- "Positive content is more viral than negative content; however, this link is
  complex."
- **Arousal is the real driver, not valence.** "Virality is partially driven by
  physiological arousal. Content that evokes high-arousal positive (awe) or
  negative (anger, anxiety) emotion is more viral."
- High-arousal, ACTIVATING emotions increase sharing:
  - **Awe** (positive, high-arousal): more awe-inspiring content is more viral.
  - **Anger** (negative, high-arousal): more anger-inducing content is MORE likely
    to make the most-emailed list.
  - **Anxiety** (negative, high-arousal): more anxiety-inducing content is MORE
    likely to make the list.
  - **Surprise**: surprising content is more viral.
- Low-arousal / DEACTIVATING emotions suppress sharing:
  - **Sadness** (negative, low-arousal): sadness-inducing content is LESS viral.
  - Contentment (positive, low-arousal): deactivating, associated with decreased
    virality.
- **Disgust**: "There was no significant relationship between disgust and
  virality." (Note: contrast with Vosoughi 2018 where false-news replies showed
  more disgust — see file 08.)
- **Practical utility and interest independently drive sharing.** "More
  practically useful or interesting articles are more likely to make the New York
  Times' [most emailed list]." And critically: **"these results persist even
  controlling for interest and practical utility"** — i.e., the emotion/arousal
  effect is on top of the utility effect, not explained by it.

## The mechanism (from the paper's discussion)
"Emotions characterized by activation may excite people, signal that activity is
desired, and lead consumers to spread the word. Emotions characterized by
deactivation may calm people, signal that inactivity is desired, and thus reduce
social transmission."

"Our findings also suggest that social transmission is about more than just value
exchange or self-enhancement. Consistent with the notion that people share to
entertain, surprising and interesting content is highly viral. Similarly,
consistent with the notion that people share to inform others [practical value]."

## Companion paper (mechanism, causal)
Berger, J. (2011), "Arousal Increases Social Transmission of Information,"
Psychological Science, 22(7), 891-893. Showed experimentally that inducing
high-arousal emotional states (even unrelated to the content, e.g., via exercise)
INCREASES willingness to share — establishing arousal as causal, not just
correlational.

## Why it went viral itself (Berger/Milkman reflection, Social Science Space 2023)
https://www.socialsciencespace.com/2023/05/a-viral-paper-on-determining-what-makes-online-content-viral
Milkman: real data from a highly-influential real website (nytimes.com), published

little was known about what drives sharing.

## Relevance to nmajor.com breakout question
The breakout driver is HIGH-AROUSAL emotion (awe, anger, anxiety, surprise) layered
ON TOP OF practical value — not either/or. A named-incident cautionary tale about a
first-ever SEC filing over a customer-data leak is an anxiety/surprise engine
(activating), which is exactly the profile that travels. A checklist is pure
practical utility with the arousal stripped out — useful, save-worthy, but not
activating, so it does not get the transmission lift.
