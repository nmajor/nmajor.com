## JOHN CAPLES — Tested Advertising Methods (1932; multiple editions)
============================================================

WHO: John Caples, mail-order/direct-response copywriter at BBDO. Pioneer of split-run
A/B testing of ads. His core creed: TEST EVERYTHING; never trust opinion or gut.

VERBATIM/near-verbatim core principles (cross-confirmed):
- "Test everything. Never rely on opinions or gut feelings about what will work."
- On headline importance (as commonly cited from the book): roughly "80% of readers
  read only the headline" — i.e., the headline does most of the work of an ad.

THE FOUR THINGS A WINNING HEADLINE DOES (Caples' framework):
A winning headline should do at least one of:
1. SELF-INTEREST / BENEFIT — promise the strongest possible benefit to the reader.
2. NEWS — deliver genuine news (new, announcing, introducing).
3. CURIOSITY — arouse intense curiosity.
4. QUICK & EASY WAY / SPECIFICS — offer clear, compelling, specific information.
(Caples ranked SELF-INTEREST and NEWS as the strongest; CURIOSITY alone as the
weakest/riskiest because curiosity without benefit often fails to sell.)

THE SPECIFICITY RULE (one of his most durable findings):
"Specifics sell better than generalities."
Tested example pairing widely cited from his work:
- WEAKER (general): "Lose weight fast"
- STRONGER (specific): "Lose 10 pounds in 30 days"
The specific number is more believable AND more compelling than the vague claim.

HIS MOST FAMOUS HEADLINES (concrete examples, verbatim):
1. "They Laughed When I Sat Down At The Piano — But When I Started To Play!"
   (U.S. School of Music, ~1925-26). Considered one of the 100 greatest ads ever.
   WHY IT WORKS: it tells a STORY, opens a curiosity loop ("but when I started to
   play..."), and promises a TRANSFORMATION (social admiration). Story + open loop +
   implied benefit, all in one line.
2. "Do You Make These Mistakes in English?"
   WHY IT WORKS: curiosity ("these mistakes" = unnamed gap) + self-interest/fear of
   embarrassment + ultra-specific framing ("these," not "mistakes in general").

CAPLES ON STYLE (verbatim from Lars Lofgren's distillation of the book):
"Don't make your copy pithy and cute. No one will get it. They'll just think you're
boring and confusing." (Plain, clear, specific beats clever — strongly anti-hype-friendly.)

Caples' 35 headline formula buckets include: How To / Why / Which / Who Else Wants /
Warning / Announcing / Introducing / New / direct benefit / news / curiosity / price.

## DAVID OGILVY — on headlines
============================================================

WHO: David Ogilvy, founder of Ogilvy & Mather, "Father of Advertising." Wrote the
foreword to the 4th edition of Caples' Tested Advertising Methods, calling it:
VERBATIM: "This is, without doubt, the most useful book about advertising that I have
ever read."

THE MOST-CITED OGILVY HEADLINE QUOTE (verbatim):
"On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy.
When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar."

Alternate/expanded formulation Ogilvy used (verbatim):
"On the average, five times as many people read the headlines as read the body copy.
It follows that unless your headline sells your product, you have wasted 90 percent of
your money."

Ogilvy headline guidance (durable rules):
- Headlines that promise a BENEFIT are read by far more people than those that don't.
- Put the BRAND and the PROMISE/BENEFIT in the headline where possible.
- Headlines with NEWS ("new", "now", "introducing") and SPECIFICS perform strongly.
- Long, specific, informative copy can outsell short copy ("The more you tell, the
  more you sell") — when it is genuinely informative.

Ogilvy's most famous headline (often cited):
"At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric
clock." — a single, concrete, specific, UNDERSTATED detail that proves engineering
quality without any superlative or hype. (Exemplary anti-hype hook: specificity and
concrete proof in place of adjectives.)

## ANTI-HYPE ASSESSMENT
============================================================
- TRANSFERS WELL (credible-friendly): TEST EVERYTHING (humility/evidence over
  opinion); SPECIFICITY beats generality (numbers, concrete detail); benefit/self-
  interest as the primary lever; news as a hook; the Rolls-Royce "concrete
  understatement" model; Caples' plain-clear-over-clever rule.
- HANDLE WITH CARE: curiosity-only headlines (Caples himself rated them weakest and
  risky); they tip into clickbait if there's no real payoff/benefit underneath.
