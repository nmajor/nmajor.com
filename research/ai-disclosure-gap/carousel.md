**Slide 1 (hook)**
Banks mention AI least of any industry in their filings.
They also use it the most.
That gap is a disclosure checklist.

**Slide 2**
1. Separate the two decisions.
What you run is one choice. What you say about it in public is another, with different risks.
Most disclosure trouble comes from treating them as one.

**Slide 3**
2. Ask what each claim commits you to if it's wrong.
A filing, a press release, a careers page — all on the record.
Would you defend it under scrutiny?

**Slide 4**
3. Map the claim to its liability.
Does the AI touch a regulated or consumer-facing call, like credit or a coverage denial?
Are you describing more than the system actually does?

**Slide 5**
4. Match disclosure to that liability.
Banks stay quiet near lending decisions on purpose.
Insurers say more partly because a regulator makes them document it.

**Slide 6**
5. Never read saying-less as doing-less.
In the data, the quietest companies were often the heaviest users.
Assume otherwise and you're reading the wrong signal.

**Slide 7 (recap + soft CTA)**
The point isn't to go dark.
It's to know which AI claims you'd stand behind if one turned out wrong.
